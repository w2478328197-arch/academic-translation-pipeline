Some studies suggest that training at intensities near or above
the LT or OBLA pushes the LT and OBLA to the right (i.e., lactate
accumulation occurs later at a higher exercise intensity) (39, 43).
This shift probably occurs as a result of changes in hormone release,
particularly reduced catecholamine release at high exercise
intensities, and increased mitochondrial content that allows for
greater production of ATP through aerobic mechanisms. Broadly, the
training-induced adaptations result in the athlete being able to rely
more on free fatty acids to meet the ATP demands of the exercise
task. Thus shifting the LT or OBLA to the right allows the athlete to
