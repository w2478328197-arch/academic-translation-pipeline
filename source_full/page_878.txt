Activity Level
An active person tends to be more flexible than an inactive one,
although it is important to note that it is not activity level alone that
improves flexibility but also the type of activity that influences
flexibility (76). Stretching exercises, or exercises requiring the body
to move through a full ROM, are essential if joint flexibility is to be
maintained or increased. Similarly, resistance training, especially
using full ranges of motion, can be an effective stimulus to maintain
or increase ROM, and both men and women have successfully
increased their flexibility as an outcome of properly designed
resistance training programs (69).
Proprioceptor: Muscle Spindles
The muscle spindles are located within intrafusal muscle fibers that run parallel
to extrafusal muscle fibers; they monitor changes in muscle length (40). During a
rapid stretching movement, a sensory neuron from the muscle spindle innervates a
motor neuron in the spine. The motor neuron then causes a muscle action of the
previously stretched extrafusal muscle fibers; this is the stretch reflex.
Stimulation of the muscle spindle and the subsequent activation of the stretch
reflex should be avoided during stretching, because motion will be limited by the
reflexive muscle action. If the muscle spindles are not stimulated, the muscle
relaxes and allows greater stretch. Because of the very slow movement during
static stretching, the stretch reflex is not invoked. Rapid (ballistic and dynamic)
stretching movements may stimulate the muscle spindles, causing a stretch reflex.
