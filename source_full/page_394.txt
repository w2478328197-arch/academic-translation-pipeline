Two children of the same chronological age can differ
biologically by up to five years, which underlines the
importance of accounting for maturation in the design
of individualized training programs.
Muscle and Bone Growth
As children grow, muscle mass steadily increases. At birth,
approximately 25% of a child’s body weight is muscle mass, and by
adulthood this percentage increases to about 40% (146). During
puberty, marked increases in hormonal concentrations (e.g.,
testosterone, growth hormone, and insulin-like growth factor) in
boys result in a discernible increase in muscle mass and widening of
the shoulders, whereas in girls an increase in estrogen production
causes increased body fat deposition, breast development, and
widening of the hips (59). Although muscle mass in girls continues
to increase during adolescence, the increase occurs at a lower rate
than in boys due to differences in their hormonal profiles (146).
Throughout this time, the increase in muscle mass in both sexes is
due to the hypertrophy of individual muscle fibers rather than
hyperplasia (146). Peak muscle mass occurs between the ages of 16
and 20 years in females and between 18 and 25 years in males
unless affected by exercise, diet, or both (146).
