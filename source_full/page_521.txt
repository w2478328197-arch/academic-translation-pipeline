Regular Communication With All Stakeholders
Strength and conditioning professionals should maintain regular
communication with all athletic trainers, position coaches, parents (if
appropriate), and head coaches. During the initial stage of injury
rehabilitation, the strength and conditioning professional might
contribute minimally to the rehab process. To ensure optimal
transitions between phases of the rehabilitation program, the
strength and conditioning professional should be aware of the
training completed at the front end of the process. The sports
medicine team can ensure their involvement is sequential and
concurrent to prevent the risk of progressing too quickly and
possibly reinjuring (59). Additionally, strength and conditioning
professionals should maintain the injured athlete’s privacy and
confidentiality throughout the return-to-play process.
Specifically Tailored Training Programs
Depending on the severity of the injury, athletes could require a
modified training program that may require different exercises,
tempos, 
equipment, 
or 
arrangements 
(e.g., 
smaller, 
more
personalized settings). If the time and resources allow it, every effort
should be made to customize the training program to accommodate
the athlete’s current abilities and limitations.
Proper Goal Setting and Acknowledgment of Training
Milestones
During a lengthy return-to-play timeline, setbacks, frustration, and
lack of expedient progress are common. Coaches may consider
overlapping appropriate goal-setting techniques with the sports
medicine team to encourage and motivate the athlete. Goals should
be relevant to controllable factors (e.g., Did I come to training? Did I
perform ten repetitions?) and be flexible to prevent frustration from
unexpected adjustments (63).
