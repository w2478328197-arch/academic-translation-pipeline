Electromyography studies have also yielded some interesting
findings regarding neural adaptations to anaerobic training:
Exercising muscle undergoing unilateral resistance training
produces increased strength and neural activity in the
contralateral resting muscle, a phenomenon known as cross-
education (79). A literature review has shown that strength
in the untrained limb may increase up to 22%, with an
average strength increase of approximately 8% following
training periods ranging from 4 to 12 weeks (125). The
increase in strength of the untrained limb is accompanied by
greater EMG activity in that limb (154), thereby suggesting
that a central neural adaptation accounts for most of the
strength gains, considering no change or reduction in the
cross-sectional area (CSA) of muscle during the unilateral
training period (22, 139).
Unilateral strength, via cross-education, increases even when
limbs are trained at different intensities and during detraining
periods (22). The cross-education effect does appear to be
load dependent, with untrained limb strength improving when
the contralateral trained limb performs higher-load (8RM-
12RM) intensities (22). Furthermore, the untrained limb
strength improvements will also benefit if the untrained limb
performs low-load (30RM-40RM) work while the trained limb
performs high-load (8RM-12RM) synchronously, but not if the
trained limb performs traditional unilateral work under low-
load intensities (30RM-40RM) (22).
