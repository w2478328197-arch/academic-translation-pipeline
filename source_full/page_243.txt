Categories of Hormones
Hormones can be classified into three main categories based on their
molecular structure: steroid, peptide (or polypeptide), and amine
hormones.
Steroid Hormone Interactions
Steroid hormones, including cortisol, testosterone, and estradiol
produced by the adrenal cortex and/or gonads, can diffuse across
cell membranes due to their lipophilic (fat-soluble) nature. They
engage with specific receptors inside the cell to form a hormone–
receptor 
complex 
(H-RC). 
This 
complex 
undergoes 
a
conformational change, activates, and pairs with another H-RC. It
then moves to the nucleus, interacts with the DNA, and promotes
the transcription process for specific proteins by “unzipping” DNA
and identifying regulatory elements of the targeted gene (18, 117).
The resulting mRNA is then translated by ribosomes in the
sarcoplasm into the intended proteins. The steroid hormone–induced

Testosterone’s influence on muscle cells encompasses more than
its genomic effects. In addition to the primary nuclear receptor, a
membrane receptor for testosterone has also been described in
detail (128). Such a membrane receptor allows testosterone to affect
the cell through rapid and nongenomic interactions (i.e., those that
do not involve DNA transcription in the nuclei) within various other
cellular compartments. These interactions play a crucial role in
muscle adaptation, recovery, and growth by facilitating the
production 
of 
essential 
contractile 
and 
structural 
proteins,
modulating calcium flux in muscle cells, altering membrane fluidity,
and activating key signaling pathways (e.g., the MAPK/ERK
pathway). Such nongenomic actions underscore testosterone’s
multifaceted 
role 
in 
muscle 
development.
