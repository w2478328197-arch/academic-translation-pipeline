Interpretation
Speed
The relationship between steps
and the sum of the first step’s
ground contact time and the
flight time leading to the
subsequent ground contact.
Alternatively, instantaneous
speed or velocity can be
examined using GPS technology,
LiDAR, laser, radar, motorized
resistance devices, and 2D and
3D motion capture.
Speed dictates how fast an athlete can
move the body mass over a specific
distance.
Instantaneous speed/velocity at time
points/distances can be calculated,
facilitating a greater insight into sprint
strategy
Acceleration
The change in speed as
determined by the delta of two
steps (e.g., right heel to left
heel) and the sum of ground
contact times and flight times of
each step.
Acceleration provides insight into how
well the athlete changes speed
(increasing velocity in a direction) over a
prescribed distance. For most athletes,
furthering the length an athlete can
accelerate will lead to higher top speed,
provided that postural integrity and
mechanics are sound.
Technique
Acceleration and maximum
velocity techniques can be
objectively quantified (i.e., joint
angles) or qualitatively
examined and compared to
technical models to help
improve performance and
mitigate injury risk.
Qualitative analysis is relatively simple to
perform and only requires filming of
sprint technique. High-speed video
recording is a common feature of most
smartphones and tablets, and athletes’
technique can be compared to technical
models such as Altis Kinogram or Sprint-
Mechanics assessment score (16) to help
individualize training.
Note: ETG = electronic timing gate; 2D = two-dimensional; 3D = three-dimensional; LiDAR
= light detection and ranging.
