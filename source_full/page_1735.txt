Satellite Training Facilities
It has become common in recent years for college and professional
sport organizations to add alternative and sport-specific satellite
training facilities to accommodate the needs of growing strength and
conditioning departments. Other examples of this include providing a
strength and conditioning facility for visiting team use, as is
mandated by some professional sport leagues, or designing a studio
space for individualized or small group training sessions. Special
attention should be given when planning satellite training facilities,
especially if existing, dated, or nontraining spaces are being
converted for strength and conditioning use.
Satellite training facilities may benefit from smaller pieces of
equipment based on the size of the space available (e.g., half rack
vs. full rack). In small facilities, it can be beneficial to install
multipurpose pieces of equipment rather than equipment with a
single function (9). A satellite training facility that does not have
dedicated walkways separating the equipment areas should be set
up with equipment bolted and secured around the perimeter. The
center of the room should remain open for transitions between
training stations while allowing unimpeded access to emergency
exits. As a rule, equipment and safety spacing should never be
compromised due to limited space. In accordance with the NSCA
Strength and Conditioning Professional Standards and Guidelines
(16), and as explained in this chapter, a minimally compliant
facility must be in agreement with mandated building codes and
regulations, including accessibility (i.e., wheelchair access and ADA
compliance), HVAC, electrical, lighting, flooring, storage, square
footage, safety spaces, and overhead clearance and must possess
well-maintained equipment and surfaces.
