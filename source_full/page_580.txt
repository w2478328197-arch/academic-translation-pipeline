Vitamin D is a particular vitamin of interest for both sedentary and
athletic individuals because it is important for immunity, protein
synthesis, muscle function, cardiovascular function, inflammatory
response, cell growth, and regulation of muscle and bone (39, 121).

Dietary Guidelines Advisory Committee, more than 90 percent of
men and women over 19 years of age do not consume enough
vitamin D (56, 154). Eating a diet containing seafood, and choosing
foods that are fortified with vitamin D, including milk, fortified soy
beverages, fortified soy yogurt, and some whole-grain cereals, can
help adults meet their needs for vitamin D. In addition to dietary
sources, the body can make vitamin D from the sun through sun
exposure on the skin; however, populations living at higher latitudes
or who spend a lot of time indoors do not have enough sun
exposure to meet vitamin D needs (51). As such, vitamin D
supplementation may be necessary for many individuals to prevent
deficiency. A meta-analysis of 11 randomized control trials and 436
athletes showed that vitamin D supplementation only had a small or
trivial effect on upper and lower body muscular strength or power
(150). Despite lack of a notable performance effect, adequate
vitamin D levels may be important for athletes to protect against
stress fractures and acute illness (35, 39, 91).
