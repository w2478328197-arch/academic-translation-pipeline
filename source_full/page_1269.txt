Program Design
Plyometric exercise prescription is similar to resistance and aerobic
exercise prescriptions—mode, intensity, frequency, duration, volume,
density, technique development and progression, and a warm-up
period must all be dynamic variables incorporated in the design of a
sound plyometric training program. Unfortunately, there is minimal
research demarcating optimal program variables for the design of
plyometric 
exercise 
programs. 
Therefore, 
when 
prescribing
plyometric exercise, strength and conditioning professionals must
rely on the available research, practical experience, and the
increasing availability of motion capture and force plate technology
to guide decision making.
Needs Analysis
To properly design a plyometric training program, the strength and
conditioning professional must analyze the needs of the athlete by
evaluating his or her sport, sport position, anthropometry, and
training status. Each sport and position has its own unique
requirements; some requirements are unique because of the
movements involved, while others have distinctive injury profiles and
risks. The anthropometry of the athlete is important, because
heavier athletes face exponentially higher loads in gravity-assisted
exercises than athletes with a lower mass; therefore, athletes’ body
weight and their strength relative to their body weight become
important considerations. Further, each athlete possesses a unique
training status; some may be new to training and have never
performed plyometric exercises, while others may have been injured.
Each of these populations of athletes requires a different approach
to plyometric training. By understanding each sport’s individual
requirements, the positions within the sport, and the needs of each
athlete, the strength and conditioning professional is better able to
design a safe, effective plyometric training program.
