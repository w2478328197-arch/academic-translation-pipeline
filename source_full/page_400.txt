better to underestimate their physical abilities and gradually increase
the volume and intensity of training than to exceed their abilities and
risk injury or long-term negative health outcomes.
Responsiveness to Resistance Training in Children
A plethora of research now shows both children and adolescents can
make meaningful improvements in a wide range of athletic qualities
in response to different modes of resistance training (59, 141). Much
of the early controversy surrounding youth resistance training
stemmed from the issue of children’s trainability (i.e., children’s
responsiveness to the stimulus of resistance exercise) due to early
studies failing to demonstrate a strength increase in preadolescents
who participated in a resistance training program (49). Although the
lack of significant findings in these studies could be explained by
methodological shortcomings, such as short study duration or
inadequate training volume or intensity, the results from these
reports are sometimes cited as proof that resistance training is
ineffective for children. As previously discussed, muscular strength
normally increases from childhood and throughout the teenage
years; thus, a more appropriate conclusion from these reports may
be that training-induced gains from a short-duration, low-volume,
and low-intensity training program are not distinguishable from gains
attributable to normal growth and maturation.
