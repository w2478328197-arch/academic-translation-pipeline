The hydrogen atoms are passed down the chain (a series of
electron carriers known as cytochromes) to form a proton
concentration gradient, which provides the energy for ATP
production, with oxygen serving as the final electron acceptor
(resulting in the formation of water). Due to NADH + H+ and FADH2
entering the ETC at different sites, they differ in the amount of ATP
produced. One molecule of NADH can produce 2.5 molecules of ATP,
whereas one molecule of FADH2 can only produce 1.5 molecules of
ATP (however, traditional biochemistry often cites that NADH yields 3
ATP and FADH2 yields 2 ATP [34a]). The production of ATP during
this process is referred to as oxidative phosphorylation. The
oxidative system, beginning with glycolysis and including the Krebs
cycle and ETC, results in the production of approximately 32 ATP
from the degradation of one molecule of blood glucose (12a, 117a).
However, if the initiation of glycolysis is muscle glycogen, the net
ATP production is slightly higher at 34 ATP, using the malate-
aspartate shuttle system or 32 ATP using the glycerol 3-phosphate
shuttle system because the first ATP consuming step in glycolysis is
bypassed (12a, 117a). Nevertheless, oxidative phosphorylation
accounts for over 90% of ATP synthesis compared to substrate-level
phosphorylation, which demonstrates the capacity of energy transfer
by the oxidative system. See table 3.1 for a summary of these
processes.
