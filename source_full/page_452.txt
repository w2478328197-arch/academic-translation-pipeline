Metabolic
The metabolic system is a key system in physical activity, and
understanding its function is necessary to understanding the acute
responses and training adaptations of the other body systems. While
the metabolic system operates similarly in males and females, some
differences exist that relate to the amount and type of muscle mass,
which can affect the magnitude of training-related adaptations.
However, there is a lack of research comparing males and females of
similar training backgrounds, as well as in high-level athletes, which
limits the understanding of the metabolic system and how it adapts
in female athletes.
Anaerobic Energy
The biochemical pathways that provide short-term energy include
ATP-PCr and glycolysis. While all muscle fibers can produce energy
with all types of biochemical pathways, the faster muscle fibers that
have a greater capacity for force production have a greater amount
of the substrates (phosphocreatine, glycogen) and enzymes needed
for the nonoxidative biochemical pathways (see chapter 3). On
average, males have a slightly higher proportion of these faster
(type II) muscle fiber types (84, 165). While both males and females
have the capacity to undergo muscle hypertrophy with various types
of anaerobic training, as previously indicated, the level of absolute
hypertrophy attained by males will exceed that of females, resulting
in greater storage potential for phosphocreatine and glycogen,
creatine kinase, and glycolytic enzymes, as well as greater overall
glycolytic capacity (54, 155).
