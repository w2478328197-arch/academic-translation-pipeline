The greater the athlete’s body weight, the more stress is placed on
muscles, connective tissues, and joints. External load in the form of
bars, weight vests, ankle weights, and wrist weights can be added to
the body to increase a drill’s intensity.
Much like exercise selection for resistance training, specific
consideration should be given to the muscle actions that are
incorporated into each plyometric exercise because this will alter the
muscle activation and resulting intensity. Reactive exercises require
athletes to reduce forces in a landing impact prior to propelling
themselves in the desired direction, while ballistic exercises may not
(37). For example, a single countermovement jump does not require
an athlete to absorb the same force as a depth jump. This realization
is important, because research indicates that muscle activation,
power, and relative impulse are all higher in rebound exercises
compared to ballistic exercises, which demonstrates an increased
level of intensity (37).
When considering exercise intensity, it is important to take note of
the exercise intensity overall as well as at the joint level. Research
has shown that intensity is joint specific when considering the hips,
knees, and ankles of several exercises (61). For example, the
intensity of a backward jump is higher at the hips and ankles than it
is at the knees. Exercise modifications based on the joint
considerations of an athlete are not uncommon, and considering the
intensity applied at each joint that is involved in an exercise will
likely result in more effective programming and adaptation.
