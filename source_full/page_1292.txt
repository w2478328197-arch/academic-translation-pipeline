However, many plyometric activities can be safely taught to athletes
without such formalized levels of strength (figure 19.5). It is our
recommendation that plyometrics be included for all athletes whose
sports require running, landing, jumping, or cutting. As a
prerequisite, teaching proper alignment and movement mechanics
through the use of plyometric exercise has been shown repeatedly
to decrease the athlete’s risk of injury during practices and games
(50). Various authors have theorized that basic in-place jumps such
as repeated pogo jumps and countermovement jumps require
relatively low levels of strength; repeated hops and jumps may
require a basic strength level equivalent to a back squat of 1 × body
weight, bounding and depth jumps using a box 12 inches (30 cm)
tall or shorter may have a back squat strength equivalent of 1.5 ×
body weight, and depth or box jumps with a box higher than 12
inches (30 cm) may require a back squat strength equivalent of 2 ×
body weight (58).
