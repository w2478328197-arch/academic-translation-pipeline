How to Calculate a Training Load If an athlete’s training goal is muscular
strength and the tested 1RM in the bench press exercise is 220 pounds (100 kg),
to increase strength, the athlete needs to handle loads of at least 85% of the
1RM (after warm-up) that typically allow performance of up to 6 repetitions
within a single set (table 18.11). More specifically, if the strength and
conditioning professional assigns 4 repetitions for this exercise, the
corresponding load could be approximately 90% of the 1RM (table 18.8), or
approximately 200 pounds (91 kg). Note that the strength and conditioning
professional should make adjustments to the assigned loads based on
observation of the ease or difficulty an athlete experiences in lifting the load for
the required repetitions. Moreover, it is important to avoid training to failure on a
regular basis because it may not be necessary to maximize muscular
hypertrophy, strength, or power adaptations (36, 63, 137, 191). By minimizing
training to failure, strength and conditioning professionals may reduce the
overall training strain on athletes while still providing an effective training
stimulus and maximizing performance gains (17, 18).
Assigning Percentages for Power Training The force–velocity curve
illustrates that the greater the amount of concentric muscular force generated,
the slower the muscle shortening and corresponding movement velocity (and
vice versa). Maximal power, in contrast, may be produced at intermediate
velocities with the lifting of light to moderate loads in nonballistic, traditional
resistance training exercises such as the squat or bench press (27, 105, 154,
157). Performing a 1RM involves slower movement velocities; near-maximal
forces are generated but with reduced power output (56, 197). Seldom is an
athlete required to demonstrate a singular, maximal, slow-speed muscular
strength effort in a sport (except in powerlifting, for example). Most sport
movements are faster and involve higher power outputs than those produced
during a 1RM test (89, 118). This does not mean that an athlete’s power
capabilities are unaffected by maximal muscular strength training, however.
