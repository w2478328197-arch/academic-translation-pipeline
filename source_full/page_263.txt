Insulin-Like Growth Factors and Binding
Proteins
IGFs, primarily IGF-1 and IGF-2, are small polypeptides crucial for
various anabolic processes in the body, including cellular growth,
development, and metabolism. These growth factors are structurally
similar to insulin and originate from larger precursor molecules
modified posttranslationally to become active. IGF-1, the most
researched, is predominantly synthesized in the liver in response to
stimulation by 22 kD GH (16, 22, 27, 91). Besides GH, IGF synthesis
is also influenced by thyroid hormones and testosterone (137, 138,
139, 140).
IGFs perform several functions, including enhancing protein
synthesis, increasing amino acid transport across cell membranes,
and stimulating growth in tissues such as muscle and bone. In the
bloodstream, IGFs are mostly bound to proteins, with only about 2%
in the free (unbound) form; same as for other hormones, the
binding proteins regulate IGF activity and availability. These binding
proteins are critical in modulating the growth-promoting actions of
the IGFs (91). Additionally, IGFs stimulate the secretion of their
binding proteins within muscle cells, influencing cellular sensitivity to
IGFs (105). IGF binding proteins (IGFBPs) are a group of proteins
that regulate the bioavailability and activity of IGFs. There are six
main types of IGFBPs (IGFBP-1 to IGFBP-6) found in humans, each
with distinct binding affinities, tissue distributions, and biological
functions. IGFBPs can inhibit or enhance the action of IGFs by doing
the following:
1. Binding IGFs with high affinity, which prevents their interaction
with IGF receptors and modulates their half-life, transport, and
tissue localization
2. Regulating IGF turnover and clearance in the circulation
