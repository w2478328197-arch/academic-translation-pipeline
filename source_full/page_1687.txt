As can be seen in this definition, this is a serious condition for the
athlete from which recovery can take a long time, and it greatly
interferes or halts the competition training program. Since this
definition was proposed, more recent working groups have added
their definitions to the literature, as well as concerns with the state
of the overtraining research (65, 135) that have helped illustrate the
challenges of understanding this phenomenon.
An important aspect of this definition that was also clarified in
these position stands is that overtraining is actually the process
(e.g., excessively high volumes and intensities of training) that then
can result in an overtraining syndrome (i.e., the collection of
symptoms and performance impairments) (figure 24.4) (88, 89). To
be overtrained, one actually has the OTS and the required long-term
performance impairments (i.e., weeks to months or longer).
Performance impairments are necessary for overtraining to have
occurred. However, we will see that not all performance impairments
are alike.
