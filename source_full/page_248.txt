Training-Mediated Hormonal Responses
and Mechanisms
Electrical, chemical, and hormonal signals are transmitted from the
brain, active muscles, and other tissues to various endocrine glands,
preparing the body’s physiological systems for the demands of the
workout (20, 23, 28, 32, 35, 49, 75, 80, 81). Heavy resistance
training over months to years significantly enhances musculature
size, strength, and power, mediated partly through adaptive
hormonal responses (47, 50, 70, 73, 76, 88, 95). These adaptations
arise from increased levels of anabolic hormones such as
testosterone, growth hormone, and insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-
1) that acutely rise during exercise and remain elevated after
exercise. Additionally, resistance exercise leads to an upregulation of
hormone receptors in muscle tissues, which increases the muscle’s
sensitivity to these hormones and enhances protein synthesis and
muscle hypertrophy. The structural changes in muscle and neural
adaptations contribute to increased strength and power, including
improvements in the rate of force production and alterations in
muscle fiber recruitment patterns. Resistance training also positively
affects bone density and the strength of connective tissues,
supporting increased muscular strength and overall physical
performance. One critical pathway in muscle protein synthesis
activated by resistance training is the mammalian target of the
rapamycin (mTOR) pathway, which is crucial for muscle growth.
These complex interactions result in adaptive changes in skeletal
muscle and other tissues, enhancing the activation of muscle
functions over time.
Acute hormonal releases reflect the nature and intensity of
physiological stress, such as insulin-related metabolic demands,
prompting adjustments in resting metabolism. These coordinated
hormonal responses are critical for adapting to training programs,
influencing tissue remodeling, and promoting anabolic actions.
