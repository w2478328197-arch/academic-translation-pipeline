Lactate Threshold and Onset of Blood Lactate
Accumulation
As exercise intensity increases, specific break points are evident in
the lactate accumulation curve (figure 3.5), indirectly representing
the change in metabolic acidosis (39, 99). The exercise intensity or
relative intensity at which blood lactate begins an abrupt increase
above the baseline concentration has been termed the lactate
threshold (LT) or (LT1) (163). The LT represents a significantly
increased reliance on the glycolytic pathway for energy production to
meet demand. The LT corresponds well with the ventilatory
threshold (breaking point in the relationship between ventilation and
V̇ O2) and is often used as a marker of the anaerobic threshold.
The LT typically begins at 50% to 60% of maximal oxygen
uptake in untrained individuals and at 70% to 80% in aerobically
trained athletes (29, 52). A second exponential increase in the rate
of lactate accumulation has been noted at higher relative intensities
of exercise. This second point of inflection has been termed the
onset of blood lactate accumulation (OBLA) or lactate
threshold (LT2) and occurs approximately when the concentration
of blood lactate reaches 4 mmol/L (85, 138, 144). Alternatively, the
LT2 threshold can be calculated, with the Dmax modified method
reported to have the highest repeatability (81). The breaks in the
lactate accumulation curve may correspond to the points at which
intermediate and large motor units are recruited during increasing
exercise intensities (94). The muscle cells associated with large
motor units are typically type II fibers, which are particularly suited
for anaerobic metabolism and lactate production.
