Overtraining and Overreaching
A number of definitions of overtraining and overreaching can be
found in the literature; however, in many cases the definitions are
vague or extremely general. Some may think the concept of
overtraining is a relatively new topic. However, as previously
mentioned, though not in the context of sport performance, the
issue was recognized as early as 1866 concerning general physical
effort. It was stated that “the exertion shall not exceed the powers
of recruitment; and … the recruitment shall be facilitated by
adequate diet and rest” (101). Additionally, much of the overtraining
literature is based on the seminal work of Hans Selye’s GAS (117),
which is also discussed in chapter 22. In the 1980s and 1990s, sport
scientists and sports medicine clinicians began to recognize the
problems of overtraining in sport and began to study and evaluate
this challenge as described in several reviews (13, 38, 72, 74, 80,
104, 126, 129, 139). The following things had become clear:
Not all forms of overtraining are identical (72, 129).
Not all types of performance decrements are the same (33,
34, 80, 104).
The contributing factors are variable (13, 38, 72, 80, 104, 126,
129, 139).
