Dietary Supplements
The global market for sports nutrition products continues to grow at
a significant pace, with an estimated market value of over US$53.6
billion (208). Some sports nutrition companies make unsubstantiated
claims concerning the efficacy of their products. At times,
unscrupulous companies have knowingly placed analogs of banned
substances in their products to enhance their effects (44). As a
result, much confusion has been created among athletes regarding
appropriate supplements and the ethics of companies. This section
describes dietary supplements that are commonly used by athletes
and presents scientific examination of their efficacy.
Essential Amino Acids, Branched-Chain
Amino Acids
Essential amino acids (EAAs) are not produced in the body and
must be obtained through the diet. They include histidine,
isoleucine, leucine, valine, lysine, methionine, phenylalanine,
threonine, and tryptophan. High levels of EAAs can be found in any
number of animal-based proteins or as supplements sold over the
counter. Leucine can also be found in plants such as spinach and
broccoli, but the amount of leucine is minimal when compared to
that in animal-based sources.
Efficacy

acids as well as individual amino acids and their role in stimulating
muscle protein synthesis. For example, Tipton and colleagues (206)
