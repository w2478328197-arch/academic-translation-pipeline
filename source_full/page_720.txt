Caffeine from a food source (coffee consumption) and anhydrous
caffeine have both shown significant performance effects; however,
the extent of performance improvements appears to be greater
when caffeine is ingested in tablet form (91). When caffeine is
provided as a pure caffeine supplement, the ergogenic benefit with
respect to improved aerobic endurance performance has been
reported to range between 28% and 43% (91). However, when it is
provided in a food source such as coffee (either caffeinated coffee or
decaffeinated coffee with added caffeine), the ergogenic benefit for
aerobic endurance exercise may not be seen (91). Graham and
colleagues (91) suggested that although the bioavailability of
caffeine is the same whether it is consumed in a food source or in an
anhydrous form, some compound in coffee antagonizes the action of
caffeine.
In doses of 3 to 9 milligrams per kilogram body weight
consumed approximately 60 minutes before exercise or
during prolonged exercise, caffeine is ergogenic. No
further benefit is observed when it is consumed at
higher dosages (≥9 mg/kg). It can decrease feelings of
perceived exertion, improve work capacity, and increase
mental alertness. The scientific literature does not
support caffeine-induced diuresis during exercise or any
harmful change in fluid balance that would negatively
affect performance.
