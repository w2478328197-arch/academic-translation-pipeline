Potential Risks and Concerns
Appropriately prescribed youth resistance training programs are
relatively safe when compared with other sports and activities in
which children and adolescents regularly participate (139, 181).
Paradoxically, it seems that the forces placed on the joints of young
athletes during sport participation may be far greater, and more
difficult to anticipate, than those generated from resistance training
programs (65). The belief that resistance training is dangerous for
children is not consistent with the needs of children and the
documented risks associated with this type of training. Children and
adolescents have been injured in the weight room; however, such
injuries are more likely to be accidental (98, 169) and typically occur
in cases in which the levels of supervision and instruction, technical
competency, and training loads are inappropriate (65). This
highlights the need for strength and conditioning professionals to
heed safety guidelines when working with younger populations.
Although epiphyseal plate fractures have been reported in
adolescents who were resistance training, these reports were case
studies and typically involved the performance of heavy overhead
lifts in unsupervised settings (97, 203, 207). An epiphyseal plate
fracture has not been reported in any prospective youth resistance
training study that adhered to established training guidelines (65).
