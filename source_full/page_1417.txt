Although the time required for staging the subsequent ground
contact is similar between elite and novice sprinters, elite sprinters
are able to propel themselves farther down the track due to properly
directed vertical forces. Mann (62) suggests that these vertical forces
are better directed toward the track due to an optimized knee height
at maximal flexion of the recovering leg. This higher knee position
provides a greater time period for force production and subsequent
ground clearance (i.e., range of motion force movement principle).
This technical advantage may be further evidence for why elite
sprinters tend to produce most of their force within the first half of
the stance phase.
Additionally, faster sprinters are able to achieve higher velocities
through the continuous application of high forces in a short stance
phase, which results in longer steps or steps occurring at a higher
rate. Within the elite setting (e.g., 100 m world championship
finalists), this results in male sprinters achieving velocities between
11.0 meters per second and 12.5 meters per second (24.6-28.0
mph) (46). Conversely, team sport athletes generally achieve
maximum velocities of approximately 8.0 to 9.5 meters per second
