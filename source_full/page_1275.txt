The intensity of plyometric drills is, therefore, multifactorial and
should be considered on a continuum from low to high intensity
depending on a number of planning factors. For example, skips are
considered a relatively low-intensity drill, while depth jumps are
considered a higher intensity drill because they place a higher stress
on the muscles and joints (figure 19.2).

In addition to the type of drill, several other factors also affect
plyometric intensity (table 19.5). Generally, as intensity increases,
volume should decrease (57). Because the intensity of plyometric
exercise can vary significantly, careful consideration must be given to
choosing proper drills during a specific training cycle.
