To visualize the eccentric phase, consider a countermovement
vertical jump. Stretch reflex latency (the time from reflex
stimulation to the beginning of agonist muscle activity) largely
depends on nerve conduction velocities and therefore increases with
greater distances from the spinal cord (i.e., longer nerves) (40, 42,
44, 54). Quadriceps and gastrocnemius stretch reflexes typically
range from 20 to 30 milliseconds and from 30 to 45 milliseconds,
respectively (40, 53).
Phase II is the time between the eccentric and concentric phases
and is termed the amortization (or coupling) phase. This is the
time from the end of the eccentric phase to the initiation of the
concentric muscle action. There is a delay between the eccentric and
concentric muscle actions during which type Ia afferent nerves
synapse with the alpha motor neurons in the ventral root of the
spinal cord. The alpha motor neurons then transmit signals to the
agonist muscle group. This phase of the SSC is perhaps the most
crucial in allowing greater power production; its duration must be
