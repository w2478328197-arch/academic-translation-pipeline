Training Responses and Adaptations
Before resistance training programs can be properly designed,
understanding the physiological responses to a resistance training
session and adaptations to regularly performing a resistance training
program are critical. The main body systems involved are the
musculoskeletal, metabolic, and endocrine systems, which have
varying levels of response and adaptation differences between males
and females. These differences can affect how programming
priorities are set, which is important for obtaining the optimum
training results.
Musculoskeletal
Resistance training offers a multitude of benefits for females,
including the attenuation of age-related declines in bone mineral
density (89). Specifically, the stress from the mechanical loading
applied via resistance training directly increases the magnitude of
skeletal remodeling and therefore bone mass. Resistance training
can increase bone mineral density in various skeletal regions of the
body (89). It is also known that higher intensities of resistance
training promote greater degrees of osteogenesis (103) and that
preadolescence is an opportune time to participate in weight-bearing
and other loaded activities in order to enhance bone mineral density
(65, 80).
Through participation in a resistance training program, females
can also experience muscle hypertrophy. When sophisticated
techniques (e.g., computed tomography) are used to accurately
measure changes in muscle cross-sectional area, relative short-term
(up to 16 weeks) gains in muscle hypertrophy are similar between
sexes (43, 67). When the muscular development of female
