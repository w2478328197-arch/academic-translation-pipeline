Although late maturers tend to eventually catch up to the early
maturers as they proceed through adolescence, young athletes
should realize that many factors, including motivation, coaching, and
innate ability, contribute to success in sport. Importantly, when
coaching youth populations, especially young children (e.g., 5-7
years of age), training environments should attempt to foster task-
oriented views of success (e.g., application of effort, learning
something new) rather than an ego-oriented view (e.g., simply
winning or beating others). While not an exhaustive list, previous
literature has noted that athletic development programs should seek
to cultivate a growth mindset, self-determined motivation, perceived
competence, confidence, and resilience as key psychological
attributes in young athletes (175).
Youth Resistance Training
Clinicians, coaches, and exercise scientists now agree that resistance
exercise is a safe and effective method of conditioning for children
(13, 56, 65, 71, 127, 139, 215). An increasing number of boys and
girls are participating in resistance training activities, and major
sports medicine organizations support children’s participation in a
range of resistance exercise modes, provided that the programs are
appropriately designed and supervised by qualified professionals (21,
56, 139, 215). National standards and grade-level outcomes for
physical education include guidelines and recommendations that
recognize the importance of fitness activities that enhance muscle
and bone strength (52). Perhaps most notably, the World Health
Organization stipulates that children should engage in muscle- and
bone-strengthening activities three times per week as part of their
physical activity recommendations (235).
