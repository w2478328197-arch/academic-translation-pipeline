Yoke walk: The yoke walk is another common strongman
event whereby a heavy frame is carried on the shoulders of
the athlete over a set distance. As McGill and colleagues (87)
observed significantly greater spinal compression forces during
the performance of the yoke walk when compared to six other
strongman events, it is likely that this exercise has the
potential to challenge and improve core stability. However, it
must be performed with appropriate loads and technique to
reduce the risk of injury, especially since Winwood and
colleagues (154) reported that 213 strongman athletes ranked
this exercise as the second most dangerous strongman lift in
terms of injury risk. Similar to the farmer’s walk (71), faster
yoke walks were characterized by increased stride length and
stride rate, lower limb range of motion, and reduced ground
contact time (61). Based on the impulse–momentum
relationship, it would appear that weighted carries (e.g.,
farmer’s and yoke walks) require the production of high levels
of anterior–posterior and vertical forces over short contact
times, perhaps suggesting some potential to improve
horizontal motion such as acceleration sprinting or initiating
body contact in tackling or blocking opponents in contact or
combat sports.
