3. Without a preparatory or stutter step, the athlete performs a countermovement by quickly
flexing the knees and hips, moving the trunk forward and downward, and swinging the arms
backward (figure 14.8a). During the jump, the dominant arm reaches upward while the
nondominant arm moves downward relative to the body.
4. At the highest point in the jump, the athlete taps the highest possible vane with the fingers of
the dominant hand (figure 14.8b). The score is the vertical distance between the height of the
highest vane tapped during the standing vertical reach and the vane tapped at the highest
point of the jump.
5. The best of three trials is recorded to the nearest 0.5 inches or 1 centimeter (the distance
between adjacent vanes).

The strength and conditioning professional should be aware of potential sources of error with the
jump-and-reach-style tests. These include a disconnect between the moment when the athlete
reaches the wall or vanes with the hand and the moment the maximum height of the body’s center
of mass is achieved; additionally, shoulder mobility issues may limit some athlete’s reach height.
Also, jump height values calculated using common technologies such as force plates and contact
mats are most often measured as the maximum total body center of mass height relative to its
height at takeoff and not relative to its height when standing still (158). Thus, jump height values
from jump-and-reach tests should not be compared directly to, or used interchangeably with, jump
height values recorded with such technologies or, indeed, with different jump techniques, such as
when keeping arms akimbo.
