Maximal oxygen uptake is the greatest amount of oxygen that
can be used at the cellular level for the entire body. Maximal oxygen
uptake has been found to correlate well with the degree of aerobic
conditioning and is recognized as the most widely accepted measure
of cardiorespiratory fitness (14). The capacity to use oxygen is
largely related to the ability of the heart and circulatory system to
transport oxygen. However, muscular characteristics such as muscle
fiber type, muscle fiber mitochondrial density, and capillary density in
muscle tissue also affect maximal oxygen uptake. Resting oxygen
uptake is estimated at 3.5 mL of oxygen per kilogram of body weight
per minute (mL · kg−1 · min−1) for an average person; this value is
defined as 1 metabolic equivalent (MET). Maximal oxygen
uptake values in normal, healthy individuals generally range from 25
to 80 mL · kg−1 · min−1, or 7.1 to 22.9 METs, and depend on a
variety of physiological parameters, including age and conditioning
level (17).
Oxygen uptake (V̇ O2) can be calculated with the Fick equation,
which expresses the relationship of cardiac output, oxygen uptake,
and arteriovenous oxygen difference:
V̇ O2 = Q̇  × a-vO2 difference    (1.2)
where Q̇  is the cardiac output in milliliters per minute and a-vO2
difference is the arteriovenous oxygen difference (the difference
in the oxygen content between arterial and venous blood) in
milliliters of oxygen per 100 mL of blood. Recalling equation 1.1, we
can calculate oxygen uptake as shown in the following example:
V̇ O2 = Heart rate × Stroke volume × a-vO2 difference
