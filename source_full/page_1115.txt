Programming and Accentuated Eccentric Loading
When implementing AEL as part of a periodized resistance training
program it is important that the strength and conditioning
professional remember that AEL is an advanced training tactic that is
used to exploit the established benefits of eccentric overload training
(131). As such, this training tactic should be used with caution, and
it is important that the athlete’s resistance training experience and
strength levels are considered when determining the exercises that
AEL is implemented with and the loads used during both the
eccentric and concentric phases of the movement (91).
Accentuated eccentric loading (AEL) is an advanced
training tactic and should be reserved for athletes with
extensive resistance training experience.
When programming AEL exercises the magnitude of the eccentric
load is generally considered as either being submaximal (i.e.,
<100% of concentric 1RM), maximal (i.e., 100% of concentric 1RM),
or supramaximal (i.e., >100% of concentric 1RM) (91, 143). The
decision about which eccentric load to apply is likely mitigated by the
targeted performance outcome and by the athlete’s training
experience and their relative strength levels, with stronger athletes
likely experiencing greater benefits from training with AEL tactics
(91).
One approach for implementing submaximal AEL is to apply this
loading tactic with kettlebells, dumbbells, or elastic bands while
performing plyometric exercises (e.g., countermovement, box, drop,
and depth jumps) (figure 17.6) (1, 91). For example, when a 20-
kilogram (44 lb; ~20% of body mass) load is applied with dumbbells
that are held during the eccentric phase and released prior to the
concentric phase of a countermovement jump, jump performance is
acutely enhanced (122). Similarly, when an eccentric load that is
