Prevention of rhabdomyolysis is best achieved by proper exercise
prescription. Avoidance of excessive or unaccustomed exercise
intensity, volume, and duration as well as consideration of baseline
fitness levels before engaging in new exercise serves as a key
preventative measure.
Sickle Cell Trait
The sickle cell trait (SCT) is an inherited condition characterized by
one abnormal hemoglobin gene and one normal hemoglobin gene.
Individuals with SCT may experience complications akin to sickle cell
disease under specific circumstances like extreme physical exertion
or low oxygen levels (72). Unlike sickle cell disease, which
necessitates inheriting two abnormal genes, SCT carriers usually do
not exhibit severe symptoms. In situations such as high-altitude
training environments or extreme exertion where oxygen levels are
low, the red blood cells can change to an abnormal shape (sickling)
and begin to clump together, which ultimately may be responsible
for constriction of the blood vessels. When sickling and the
associated vascular occlusion occurs, reports of fatigue or tiredness,
pain, swelling of hand and feet, and vision problems may occur (69).
Longer term consequences of sickling include damage to the blood
vessels; a shorter life span of existing red blood cells, leading to
anemia; and, in cases of vascular occlusion, stroke. Evidence
suggests that the SCT is associated with sudden death during
periods of extreme exertion and is more common among young
African Americans (69). A majority of deaths attributed to the SCT
have occurred during military training and athletic participation at
the collegiate level (69).
Should an individual develop symptoms of sickling, they should
immediately discontinue physical activity and be removed from
extreme temperature environments. Owing to potential dehydration,
proper hydration protocols should be implemented immediately (95).
Immediate referral to a medical professional is indicated if acute
symptoms are present.
