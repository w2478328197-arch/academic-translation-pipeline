Sleep
A majority of athletes receive less-than-age-appropriate sleep levels,
and a loss in sleep for athletes is a known risk factor for increased
injury risk (15, 16, 157). Female collegiate athletes may be
particularly likely to have low sleep durations that are consistent with
sleep deprivation (<7 h/night), in association with daytime
sleepiness and dysfunction (30, 69). Sleep quantity (113, 116) and
quality have been shown to be lower after training and competition
in female athletes (134, 136). Education on sleep hygiene for
athletes, coaches, and support practitioners has been shown to
address some barriers preventing good sleep quality and thus has
been shown to improve sleep in athletes (115, 135). Improving sleep
quality and quantity is critical for optimal recovery (178).
