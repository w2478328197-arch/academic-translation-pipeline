have reported novel data showing that prepubertal female gymnasts
significantly improved muscle thickness (as measured by 2D
ultrasound) in addition to measures of isometric peak force and
stretch-shortening cycle function following exposure to a 10-month
neuromuscular training intervention (161). While muscle thickness is
not a direct measure of muscle size or volume, it has been positively
correlated with muscle anatomical cross-sectional area (77). The
data from Moeskops and colleagues (161) indicate that some change
in muscle architecture might be possible even in prepubertal children
when they are exposed to long-term neuromuscular interventions.
During and after puberty, training-induced gains in strength are
typically associated with gains in muscle hypertrophy and
architecture (85, 195) due to hormonal influences and ongoing
neural adaptations (86). Although lower levels of testosterone in
adolescent females limit the magnitude of training-induced increases
in muscle hypertrophy, other hormone and growth factors (e.g.,
growth hormone and insulin-like growth factor) may be at least
partly responsible for their muscle development. Figure 7.3 highlights
the factors that contribute to the development of muscular strength
—namely, fat-free mass, testosterone concentrations, nervous
system development, and the differentiation of fast-twitch and slow-
twitch muscle fibers.
