Due to the potentially more awkward and unpredictable nature of
strongman training compared to traditional resistance training,
muscle activation and injury risk may also differ substantially
between these training approaches. McGill and colleagues (87)
quantified trunk and hip muscle activity and kinetics across the
farmer’s walk, suitcase carry (unilateral farmer’s walk), yoke walk,
tire flip, log lift, keg lift, and stone lift. While all of these movements
required exceedingly high levels of activation of certain trunk and hip
muscles and produced large spinal loads, these biomechanical
characteristics exhibited considerable between-exercise variation.
Such results suggest that each of these strongman exercises impose
somewhat unique challenges on the trunk and hip musculature,
suggesting that the inclusion of a variety of strongman exercises
may be useful to better develop spinal stability across a range of
movement situations.
Regarding injury risk, the somewhat unique challenges imposed
by different strongman exercises (87) may be consistent with the
views of 213 strongman athletes who felt that certain exercises (tire
flip, yoke walk, and stone lift) were more dangerous and likely to
contribute to injury risk when compared to other strongman
exercises (158). Perhaps as a consequence of the common usage of
the tire flip, yoke walk, and stone lift by strongman athletes, two
systematic reviews have reported greater injury rates in strongman
athletes when compared to other strength sport athletes such as
weightlifters, powerlifters, and bodybuilders (74, 121). Consequently,
athletes who perform strongman exercises should focus on
performing these exercises with appropriate technique and only use
loads that allow them to maintain adequate body and implement
control.
