Conclusion
The major goal of plyometric training is to rapidly apply force to
provide an overload to the agonist muscles. Although it has been
repeatedly shown that plyometric exercise increases muscular power
for athletes in a formal training program (34, 53, 65, 67), research
has yet to determine whether mechanical or neurophysiological
adaptations account for the improvement. Plyometrics should be
considered not an end in itself but part of an appropriately
periodized program that includes strength, speed, endurance, and
mobility training. Plyometrics requires an appropriate warm-up and
should be preplanned to allow adequate recovery. After the athlete
has begun a proper strength and conditioning program, plyometric
training may be used to further develop impulses and positive
transfer of training benefits.
