Flexibility and Performance
Given the lack of a direct link between flexibility and movement
capability, optimizing flexibility in relation to the specific activity
rather than simply maximizing flexibility should be the focus of
training (68). Clearly, the direct effects of flexibility on performance
therefore depend on the types of activity undertaken, and it is
always important to remember that the most flexible athlete is not
always the most successful (84). Given that different sports and
activities have specific requirements for ROM, it is likely that optimal
levels of flexibility exist for each activity, with these related to the
specific movements required within the particular sport or activity,
together with the reduction of any potential injury risk that may
present if an athlete lacks a specific ROM (93). As well as a
consideration of the ROM itself, it is also important that the strength
and conditioning professional keep in mind the kinetic and kinematic
patterns required within the ROM, because this will also dictate the
types of development methods used. The development of greater
ROM should be linked with the requirements for enhancing
performance, and this often involves the need to apply force over
the required ROM and the ability to assume the key technical
positions required for the sport. In some instances, greater ROM can
be seen as the missing link in the athlete’s power program, because
the ability to apply force over a greater ROM can increase impulse by
extending the time over which the athlete can apply force, which in
turn contributes to enhanced performance in a range of sports (61).
Thus, the strength and conditioning professional should look at the
optimal ROM required of the athlete but also at the underlying goal
of the actions and force patterns required through this ROM, and
where greater ROM is required, this should be in tandem with the
required force capacities to ensure that the athlete is optimally
prepared for performance.
