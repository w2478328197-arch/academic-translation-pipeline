Depth jumps and hops (“shock” actions) use gravity and the athlete’s weight
to increase exercise intensity. The athlete assumes a position on a box, steps
off, lands, and immediately transitions vertically, horizontally, laterally, or to
another box. The height of the box determines the intensity of the action, and
the volume is determined by the number of foot contacts. Repetitive jumps
onto boxes or over barriers may be added in sequence.
Upper Body Plyometrics
Rapid, powerful upper body movements are requisites for several
sports and activities, including baseball, softball, tennis, golf, and
throws in track and field (i.e., the shot put, discus, and javelin). For
example, an elite baseball pitcher routinely throws a baseball at 80
to 100 miles per hour (129-161 km/h). It is important to understand
that to reach velocities of this magnitude, throwing actions have to
be initiated with the legs and require hip–shoulder separation and
high rotational velocities in the hip and thoracic spine (e.g., a
baseball pitcher’s shoulder joint must move at more than 6,000
degrees per s) (23, 26, 27, 53). Plyometric training of the shoulder
joint may not only increase pitching velocity, but it also may prevent
injury to the shoulder and elbow joints. Such upper body actions
need to be linked with impulses generated through segmental
actions initiated in the lower body if they are to be effective in the
transfer to performance.
Plyometric drills for the upper body are not used as often as those
for the lower body and have been studied less extensively, but they
are nonetheless essential to athletes who require upper body power
(52). Plyometrics for the upper body (e.g., throws and slams) are
typically ballistic in nature (table 19.3).
