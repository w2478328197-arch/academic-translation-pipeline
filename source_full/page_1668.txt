Medical Conditions
Strength and conditioning professionals must have an understanding
of medical conditions that may develop from training as well as
preexisting conditions that may increase the risk of an adverse event
from exercise. Possessing this knowledge is essential for ensuring
safe participation in exercise and appropriate discontinuation
procedures. Recognizing the early presentation and management
strategies for rhabdomyolysis, sickling from the sickle cell trait, and
temperature-induced illnesses are a necessary requirement for
training both the athletic and general populations.
Rhabdomyolysis
Rhabdomyolysis is characterized by the breakdown of skeletal
muscle tissue, leading to the release of myoglobin into the
bloodstream. Myoglobin can pose a risk to kidney function,
potentially causing kidney damage or failure if left untreated. Various
factors can trigger rhabdomyolysis, including trauma, extreme
physical exertion, and muscle compression. The severity of
rhabdomyolysis ranges from mild muscle discomfort to severe, life-
threatening complications (43). Symptoms of rhabdomyolysis may
manifest as severe muscle pain and weakness, dark urine (often

tenderness of affected muscles, alongside fatigue, nausea, vomiting,
and confusion. In severe instances, individuals may experience
dehydration, electrolyte imbalances, and acute kidney injury (43).
If rhabdomyolysis is suspected, immediately discontinue activity,
lie the client down, implement proper hydration protocols, and
closely monitor to ensure stability while awaiting further medical
assistance (10). Prompt referral to a medical professional is
necessary to prevent more serious medical complications (10).
