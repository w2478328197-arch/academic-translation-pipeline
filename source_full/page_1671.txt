Key prevention strategies for SCT involve awareness of triggers
for sickling, education, preventive measures, and regular medical
follow-up. Testing for SCT is recommended among athletes and
those engaged in intense training because SCT has been associated
with sudden death in individuals during extreme exertion. Intense
physical activity should be approached with caution, especially in
extreme conditions or high altitudes, to reduce the potential for
sickling (81). For individuals with the SCT who engage in exercise

Heat Cramps
Heat cramps are part of a continuum of heat-related injuries or
illnesses marked by dysregulation of the body’s thermoregulation
capacity (58). Heat cramps are muscle spasms or involuntary
sustained contractions of large muscle groups that may occur when
the athlete is exposed to elevated environmental temperatures
during exercise. Heat cramps may occur during or shortly after
exercise. Several factors may contribute to heat cramps, including
dehydration, depleted electrolytes, increased training load or
duration, and muscular fatigue. Although heat cramps generally
occur in hot environments, elevated core body temperature is not
always present.
When heat cramps are suspected, immediate cessation of activity
and moving the athlete to a cooler environment to lower body
temperature is a necessity. Implementing proper rehydration is
essential as are appropriate cooling measures. Passive gentle
stretching or massage to affected muscle groups may be beneficial
(58). Heat cramps may be prevented by proper hydration protocols,
use of light-weight clothing when exercising in hot environments,
and appropriate progressive conditioning in the specific training
environment to allow heat acclimation (58).
