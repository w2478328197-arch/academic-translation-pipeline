Limited research has investigated tire flipping techniques (73,
162). Keogh and colleagues (73) primarily quantified the phases and
temporal characteristics most related to successful (fast) completion
of 2 sets of 6 repetitions of a 232-kilogram (511 lb) tire flip,
separated by 3 minutes’ rest. The phases of the movement were
broken down into a first pull, second pull, transition, and push,
somewhat similar to the clean and jerk. Results suggested that
minimizing time during the second pull and push phases was most
associated with faster flips, suggesting that the tire flip may be used
as an alternative to exercises such as clean and jerk to develop total
body power. To optimize this development of total body power, the
athlete should emphasize a powerful triple extension of the lower
extremities to generate sufficient momentum for the tire to continue
to rotate upward and forward before the push phase. This will also
require the selection of appropriately sized tires that allow this rapid
triple extension and transition to the push phase so that each
repetition is performed relatively quickly (~3-4 s).
Tire Flipping (Shoulders-Against-the-Tire
Technique): Common Technical Flaws and
Corrections
Flaw: Placing the feet too close to the tire when initiating the movement.
When this occurs, athletes often have to round their back and position their
knees close to their chest in order to initiate the movement.
Correction: Have athletes move their feet away from the tire, and instruct
them to brace their spine and use a forceful triple extension of the lower
extremities that raises their chest and tire off the ground.
Flaw: Hips rise faster than the shoulders during the initial pushing motion.
This flaw is very similar to what can be seen during traditional deadlifting
with incorrect technique.
Correction: Instruct athletes to keep their hips low and drive the tire forward
rather than lifting it.
Flaw: A lifting motion is used instead of a pushing motion.
