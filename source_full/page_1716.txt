Recovery Methods and Strategies
Once overtraining or overreaching has occurred and the individual
has developed an overtraining syndrome, the most important next
step is developing a recovery plan. An important consideration is
that overtraining often has a complex and sometimes slow onset and
is not rapidly reversed. Table 24.4 lists some of the essential or
suggested measures that must be taken when overtraining occurs.
Training Factors for Recovery
Once overtraining occurs and overtraining syndrome has developed,
the first thing that must be done is a careful revision of the training
program. A rapid decrease in the training volume, intensity, or both
is typically warranted. When overreaching has occurred, training
cessation has permitted performance to return to original levels in as
little as one week (36, 37, 87, 88, 111, 139). When more serious
levels of overtraining have occurred, recovery can take as long as
four weeks after high-intensity resistance exercise (35) or months to
a year after high-volume distance running (78). In each case,
training volume or intensity was immediately decreased or removed.
It may not always be practical or desirable to cease training,
depending on each individual case. Although it has been suggested
that one week of resistance training cessation does not benefit
strength and hypertrophy gains, the training did not incorporate
training similar to overtraining or overreaching (22). Regardless,
decreasing or eliminating the training stress is mandatory when
overtraining has occurred.
