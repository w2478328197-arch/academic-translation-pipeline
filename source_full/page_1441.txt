deceleration (e.g., 5-0-5 test). Therefore, tests such as the Illinois
agility or L-run that require a bend to maintain velocity instead of an
aggressive COD could be considered tests of maneuverability (69).
With this said, table 20.3 gives examples of COD and agility tests
and drills that address different physical requirements within the
realm of “agility.” One should consider the aspect most important to
the given sport when determining COD and agility abilities and
consider testing and monitoring various types of COD abilities and
perceptual-cognitive abilities required during true agility tests.
Furthermore, tests such as the traditional 5-0-5 may be influenced
by an athlete’s linear running speed (70, 81); thus, to isolate COD
ability, the COD deficit (5-0-5 test time—10 m linear sprint time)
could be determined (70, 71). Practitioners may also want to
consider an athlete’s sprint momentum (average velocity during a
linear sprint test or 10 m approach of the traditional 5-0-5 test ×
athlete’s mass) to provide an indication of the braking demand
experienced by athletes during the 5-0-5 test to give a more
complete view of the athlete’s COD ability.
