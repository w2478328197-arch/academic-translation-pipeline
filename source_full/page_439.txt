Implications of Resistance Training for
Females
Females who regularly participate in resistance training activities can
improve their health, reduce their risk of degenerative diseases (e.g.,
osteoporosis) and their injury rates, and enhance their overall
sporting performance. Whereas in the past females may have
questioned the value of resistance training or even avoided this type
of exercise due to social stigma, evidence clearly indicates that
females are capable of tolerating and adapting to the stresses of
resistance exercise and that the benefits are substantial (93).
Furthermore, to enhance health and fitness and reduce injury rates,
it is now suggested that resistance training be an essential
component of any exercise or training program that females follow
(127, 128, 167).
Physical Changes in Early Adulthood
While differences in growth and development between boys and girls
are covered in chapter 7, a few physical developmental changes
continue into early adulthood. On average, females mature earlier
than males (105), and most measures of female growth and
maturation are complete by around age 18. Some exceptions include
the growth plates, which typically close by the age of 20, and peak
bone density (around age 30) (105). However, neither of these are
undeveloped enough to result in a higher risk of injury from training,
including activities such as high-intensity or high-load plyometrics (2,
122). The brain also continues to develop throughout the 20s, but
