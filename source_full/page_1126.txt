Weighted Carries
Strongman competitions commonly involve a range of weighted
carries including the farmer’s walk, yoke walk, or sandbag or keg
carries, with regular gym programs potentially also involving Zercher
carries (using a barbell held across the elbows on the upper
abdominals) or unilateral carries in which the load is held overhead
or by one hip (i.e., suitcase carry). A primary advantage of these
exercises is that they often involve horizontal motion and unilateral
ground contact and require coordinated activity of many muscles of
the body. Weighted carries may therefore develop total body and
trunk stability, grip strength (for farmer’s walks) as well as the ability
to produce high levels of anterior–posterior and vertical impulse to
initiate propulsion and to be able to absorb these forces on impact at
heel strike (163). These exercises therefore may mimic performance
in a range of occupational and sporting demands (62, 87) or create
substantial metabolic demands that can be exploited in a metabolic
or anaerobic conditioning circuit (55, 163). The following exercises
are common weighted carries.
Farmer’s walk: The farmer’s walk requires the athlete to
hold a load at the sides in each hand while walking forward
(87, 147). The farmer’s walk can be performed with static
loads (e.g., farmer’s bar handles or a heavy dumbbell) or
variable loads (e.g., water-filled objects) (147). Regardless of
the type of load used, the farmer’s walk offers a unique
activation pattern of the core (87), although research in the
scientific literature is very limited in supporting the use of this
