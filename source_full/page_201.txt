at relatively high intensity for very short durations, such as
resistance training, require a rapid rate of energy supply and rely
almost entirely on energy provided by the phosphagen system.
Activities that are of low intensity but long duration, such as
marathon running, require a prolonged energy supply and rely
predominantly on the energy supplied by the oxidative energy
system. The primary source of energy for activities between these
two extremes depends on the intensity and duration of the event
(table 3.2). In general, short, high-intensity activities (e.g., high-
intensity resistance training and sprinting) rely largely on the
phosphagen energy system and glycolysis under anaerobic
conditions whereby pyruvate is primarily converted to lactate,
commonly referred to as fast glycolysis. As the intensity decreases
and duration increases, the emphasis gradually shifts to slow
glycolysis and the oxidative energy system (45, 131).
