Fibroblastic Repair Phase
Once the inflammatory phase has subsided, tissue repair begins
(34, 36); the fibroblastic repair phase, also known as the
proliferative 
phase, 
is 
characterized 
by 
catabolism 
(tissue
breakdown) and replacement of tissues that are no longer viable
following injury. In an attempt to improve tissue integrity, new
capillaries and connective tissue (scar tissue) form in the area.
Fibroblasts from the inflammatory phase, with the help of growth
factors, begin to synthesize type III collagen, which is randomly
deposited along the injured structure and serves as the framework
for tissue regeneration. This newly formed tissue is weaker than the
original tissue; thus optimal strength of the new tissue is not yet
achieved. Collagen fibers are strongest when they lie longitudinally
(i.e., parallel) to the primary line of stress, yet many of the new
fibers are positioned transversely, which limits their ability to
efficiently transmit force. This phase of tissue healing begins as early
as 72 hours after injury and may last up to two months.
Maturation–Remodeling Phase
The maturation–remodeling phase of healing often overlaps with the
fibroblastic reparative phase and may begin as early as a few weeks
after injury. During the maturation–remodeling phase of healing
the weakened tissue produced during the repair phase is
strengthened (34, 36). During this phase, new collagen fibers have
shifted to a stronger type I collagen, allowing the newly formed
tissue the opportunity to improve its structure, strength, and
function. With increased loading, the collagen fibers of the newly
formed scar tissue (a collection of cells and collagen that have
repaired damaged tissue) begin to hypertrophy and align themselves
along the lines of stress (29). The thicker and more optimally aligned
collagen fibers become stronger, allowing a return to function.
