Caloric Versus Nutrient-Dense Foods
Given the rise in obesity in America, some classify the American diet as calorie
dense but nutrient poor. The Scientific Report of the 2020 Dietary Guidelines
Advisory Committee recommends a dietary pattern including a variety of nutrient-
dense foods. Though there is no standardized definition of the term nutrient
density, in general, choosing nutrient-dense foods means looking for foods based
on the nutrients such as vitamins, minerals, and fiber, as well as the healthy plant-
based compounds that they provide, whereas caloric density refers to the caloric
content of a food. Foods high in nutrient density include milk, vegetables, protein
foods, and grains, while foods high in caloric density but low in nutrient density
typically include potato chips, desserts, and candy (44). For more information on
nutrient versus caloric density, the reader is referred to the work of Drewnowski
(44).
