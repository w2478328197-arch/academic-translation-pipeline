The differences between elite and novice sprinters can be traced
to a single component. Much of the current literature on sprinting
(17, 105, 106) suggests that the amount of vertical force applied to
the ground during the stance phase at top speed may be the most
critical component to improving top speed. In addition, these greater
forces must be applied to the ground in the shortest period possible.
Application of force is needed to displace a mass. In sprinting,
step length represents the displacement of mass. Elite male sprinters
achieve a step length of 2.70 m (8.9 ft), whereas novice sprinters
display a step length of 2.56 m (8.4 ft) at maximum velocity (figure
20.6a) (62).
Since contact with the ground is needed to continue force
production and subsequent alterations in velocity, increasing the step
frequency theoretically would maximize the time available to produce
force. Elite male sprinters demonstrate step frequency near 4.63
steps per second compared to novice sprinters, who produce a
lesser step frequency of 4.43 steps per second (figure 20.6b) (62).
In other words, elite sprinters need less ground contact time with
each step to exert the effort needed to displace their mass due to
their ability to create greater impulses. Thus, these faster sprinters
also spend more time in the air. Interestingly, elite sprinters display
times to reposition the swing leg that are similar to those of their
slower counterparts (62, 106).
