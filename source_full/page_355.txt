Adaptations to Aerobic Endurance
Training
Much research has been done on training adaptations associated
with aerobic endurance exercise (5, 15, 20, 34, 42, 65, 66, 161).
Aerobic metabolism plays a vital role in human performance and is
basic to all sports, if for no other reason than recovery (156).
Metabolically, the Krebs cycle and electron transport chain are the
main pathways in aerobic energy production. Aerobic metabolism
produces far more ATP energy than anaerobic metabolism and uses
fats, carbohydrates, and (minimally) proteins as fuel sources for
generating ATP. Many sports involve interactions between the
aerobic and anaerobic metabolic systems and thus require
appropriate training. For example, soccer, lacrosse, basketball, field
hockey, and ice hockey involve continuous movement (and thus
constant aerobic demand) mixed with bursts of sprint and power
activities. Proper conditioning of the aerobic system is vital to the
athlete’s ability to sustain such activity and adequately recover within
and between exercise sessions.
What Improvements in Performance
Result From Aerobic Exercise?
The body systems affected by aerobic exercise include musculoskeletal,
cardiovascular, and respiratory. Adaptations include the following:
•  Respiratory system. Decreased submaximal respiration rate (37).
•  Cardiovascular system. Decreased heart rate for fixed submaximal
workloads (131) associated with increased stroke volume and cardiac output.
Blood (plasma) volume is also increased, supporting increased stroke volume and
cardiac output (55). Hemoglobin mass also increases but not the percentage of
hemoglobin (i.e., hematocrit), which may decrease slightly due to the relatively
greater increase in plasma volume (140).
