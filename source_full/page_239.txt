Muscle as the Target for Hormone
Interactions
Resistance exercise and training leads to muscle remodeling through
a complex network of metabolic and cellular signals. This process
involves muscle fiber damage, an inflammatory response, and the
breakdown of damaged proteins, ultimately synthesizing new
proteins integrated into muscle structures. The inflammatory phase,
which involves the immune system, including T and B cells, is
significantly influenced by hormonal activity, emphasizing the role of
neuroendocrine immunology in understanding how the nervous,
endocrine, and immune systems interact in muscle adaptation (77).
Hormones regulate the balance between protein synthesis and
degradation, playing a critical role in muscle adaptation after
resistance exercise. They stimulate the production of contractile
proteins like actin and myosin, as well as structural proteins such as
titin, which are essential for muscle function. Anabolic hormones,
including insulin, insulin-like growth factors (IGFs), testosterone, and
GH, are particularly vital because they drive tissue growth and
counteract the effects of catabolic hormones like cortisol and
progesterone, which can lead to protein degradation. Furthermore,
hormones like thyroid hormones play a supportive, permissive role,
facilitating the actions of other hormones. The potentially negative
effects of cortisol in recovery, such as inhibiting immune cell activity
and disrupting pathways like the Akt/mechanistic target of
rapamycin
