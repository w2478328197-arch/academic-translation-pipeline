FIGURE 19.3 Proper plyometric landing technique. (a) When learning to land and stop,
the shoulders are in line with the knees, which helps place the center of gravity over the
body’s base of support. The knees are over the toes; excessive inward (valgus) movement
increases the risk of lower extremity injury; weight is evenly distributed through a flat foot
to allow a balanced deceleration position. Learning to land and stop, decelerating body
mass completely, is a precursor to learning to land and rebound (i.e., reaccelerate body
mass). (b) Proper landing technique to facilitate reactionary rebound actions; the shoulders
are in line with the knees, the ankles are stiff, and athlete contacts the ground with the
midfoot and forefoot to enable a rebound acceleration to occur. Note the alignment of the
hips, knees and toes, with the feet hip-width apart to optimize vertical or horizontal force
(depending on the subsequent movement).
Back squat equivalent strength is a descriptive term used to
infer the necessary strength capacity required to tolerate the impact
stress of the exercise so that it does not acutely overreach the
athlete and become injurious. The back squat is illustrative due to
the nature of its axial load on the total body, yet it is recognized that
not every athlete measures maximal squat strength. It is important
to note that it is not simply the magnitude of strength that is
important but the athletic quality to be able to apply the force
required within the given ground contact time. Therefore, a focus on
rate of force development as well as magnitude of force
development is an important consideration within plyometric
prescription.
