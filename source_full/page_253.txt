Primary Anabolic Hormones in Muscle
Development
Key anabolic hormones that drive muscle tissue growth and
remodeling include testosterone, growth hormone, and insulin-like
growth factors (IGFs), with additional roles played by insulin and
thyroid hormones (25, 26, 27, 28, 38, 91).
Testosterone’s Role in Muscle Adaptation
Testosterone, the primary androgen affecting skeletal muscle, and
dihydrotestosterone, which acts mainly on sex-linked tissues, play
crucial roles in the body. While circulating testosterone levels
indicate anabolic activity, the actual binding of testosterone to its
receptor is vital for stimulating muscle growth. Binding to androgen
genomic and non-genomic receptors activates various signaling
pathways that enhance gene expression for muscle hypertrophy and
suppress those involved in protein degradation. Therefore, increased
testosterone levels due to increased motor unit activation indicate
metabolic demands beyond homeostatic conditions and are
associated with enhanced receptor binding (124, 133).
Testosterone also indirectly affects muscle growth by promoting
the release of growth hormone from the pituitary, which influences
muscle protein synthesis. This hormone’s relationship with growth
hormone is either permissive or synergistic in promoting protein
synthesis (103). Furthermore, testosterone affects the nervous
