the increases in testosterone, follicle-stimulating hormone, and
luteinizing hormone. Such data demonstrate that the improvements
were more likely driven by neural factors augmented by hormonal
changes rather than significant muscle growth.
Over time, factors such as training duration and experience play
critical roles in hormonal adjustments. For instance, a notable study
by Häkkinen and colleagues (49) observed small but significant
increases in resting serum testosterone levels over two years among
elite weightlifters, alongside increases in follicle-stimulating hormone
and luteinizing hormone, which are crucial in regulating testosterone
production.
Testosterone also supports the development of the nervous
system and enhances neural adaptations necessary for strength
gains, particularly in highly trained athletes (51, 52). Additionally,
training sessions that acutely elevate testosterone levels have been
linked to more remarkable resistance training adaptations (53, 119).
The effects of resistance training on androgen receptors—
testosterone’s binding sites—are emerging as an area of significant
interest, with studies showing both increases and decreases in
androgen receptor content and DNA binding, depending on the
exercise protocol and timing of tissue sampling (12, 93, 118, 132,
133, 136). For example, Cardaci and colleagues noted that androgen
receptor-DNA binding increased only after high-intensity total body
resistance exercises and not after low-intensity exercises (12).
