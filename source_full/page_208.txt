Capacity Following Endurance Training
Hendrickse and colleagues (77) tested the hypothesis that superimposing
endurance training in both older and younger highly resistance-trained men would
lead to an increase in fiber oxidative capacity and a decrease in fiber size. Eight
highly resistance-trained younger (mean age = 28 years) and seven older (mean
age = 67 years) men participated in a 10-week cycle endurance-training program
consisting of five 6-minute intervals at 75% max heart rate (HRmax) separated by
4-minute intervals at 90% HRmax, superimposed on their usual resistance training
program of at least 4 sessions per week (2 upper body, 2 lower body). Before and
after the 10 weeks of training, V̇ O2max, body fat percentage, an MRI of the upper
leg, the maximal voluntary isometric contraction torque (MVC) of the knee
extensor muscles, and a vastus lateralis muscle biopsy were taken. While there
were expected differences in anatomical cross-sectional area of the thigh muscles,
following training no relative difference was observed between younger and older
men for V̇ O2max per kilogram body mass. The imposed endurance training equally
increased the succinate dehydrogenase (a Krebs cycle enzyme) activity in both
younger and older participants and significantly increased the number of capillaries
around type I fibers. These findings suggest that incorporating endurance training
into a resistance training program can promote aerobic metabolic adaptations
without compromising muscle size and strength in both younger and older
resistance-trained people. Extending this research in the future will help to
determine whether there is a threshold for the volume and type of endurance
exercise beyond which leads to compromised muscle size and strength
adaptations.
