Figure 1.6 shows a simplistic depiction of myosin and actin in the
sarcomere. Biochemical bonds allow for myosin proteins to
aggregate into the highly organized thick myosin filament, and thick
filaments are anchored to specialized proteins in the M-line, which is
in the center of the sarcomere (the center of the H-zone). Actin
filaments are anchored to Z-lines through another set of specialized
proteins. Although both M-lines and Z-lines exist in tandem
throughout the entire myofibril, individual sarcomeres are defined as
the thick myosin and thin actin filaments as well as associated
proteins found between two Z-lines. It is also notable that six actin
filaments surround each myosin filament, and each actin filament is
surrounded by three myosin filaments. This lattice-like arrangement
enables a highly organized and efficient structure for muscular
contraction.
Each myofibril is encased by an intricate system of tubules, called
the sarcoplasmic reticulum (figure 1.5, page 5), which terminates
as vesicles in the vicinity of the Z-lines. Calcium ions are stored in
the sarcoplasmic reticulum, and muscle activation leads to the
release of these calcium stores to facilitate muscular contraction.
Discharge of an action potential (an electrical nerve impulse)
arrives nearly simultaneously from the surface to all depths of the
muscle fiber. Calcium is thus released throughout the muscle fiber,
producing a coordinated contraction of the entire cell.
