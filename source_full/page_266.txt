Training Adaptations and IGF and
Binding Proteins
The full scope of training-induced IGF-1 adaptations across different
tissues continues to be a significant area for research (91, 112).
Training adaptations from resistance exercise influence IGF levels
and their interactions at the cellular level. Resistance training
enhances the activation and expression of IGF-1 receptors, boosting
the signaling pathways essential for cell growth and survival.
However, the full scope of how different training regimens affect IGF
levels, particularly the balance between local (muscle) and systemic
(circulating) IGF, remains a rich area for research. As noted in a
study by Hatfield and colleagues, in highly resistance-trained men
and women (i.e., squat 1RM: 2.1 ± 0.4 and 1.7 ± 0.6 × body mass,
respectively), training affects the resting and acute responses of
IGF-1 and IGFBPs (54). Thus, IGF-1 and binding proteins have
training-related 
adaptations 
in 
their 
response 
patterns 
for
production, release, binding, and interactions with cellular targets
(42, 54, 91, 112, 121). Continued research is needed to better
understand the complex dynamics of IGFs in response to resistance
training, including the specific effects of exercise on different forms
of IGF and their binding proteins. This research will help optimize
training strategies and potentially inform therapeutic interventions
targeting muscle health and performance (102, 106).
