Notwithstanding the clear benefits of resistance training,
combined resistance training (i.e., strength training and plyometrics)
has been shown to be more effective than strength training in
isolation (193). Previous research has investigated the effects of 28
weeks of combined resistance training versus combined resistance
training with weightlifting on physical fitness (187) and established
lower limb injury risk factors (188) in adolescent boys. Notably, both
training groups were able to realize improvements in physical fitness
(i.e., strength, power, speed), movement competency (i.e.,
resistance training skills battery quotient), and lower limb jump-
landing mechanics (i.e., tuck jump assessment) irrespective of
program content; however, the percentage improvement in
movement competency was greater when weightlifting progressions
were included in the combined resistance training program.
Interestingly, the first 14 weeks resulted in significant gains in
movement competency and strength, while the second 14 weeks of
training resulted in small to moderate improvements in sprint speed
and indices of power, which reflect the sequence of strength and
movement competency serving as the foundations for gains in power
(12, 99).
