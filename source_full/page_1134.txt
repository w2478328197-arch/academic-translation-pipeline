(perhaps due to the concentric-dominant nature of this exercise)
may indicate that lower and upper body sled exercises could be a
key component of a resistance training program when athletes’
recovery abilities are particularly challenged, such as when they are
performing large volumes of concurrent training or competing
several times per week.
Winwood and colleagues (160) and Williams and colleagues (152)
have examined the potential of moderate to heavy sled pulls to
produce a postactivation potentiation enhancement (PAPE) sprinting
effect in adult semi-elite rugby (75%-150% body mass) and
adolescent high school soccer players (loads producing a velocity
decline of 40%-50% that equated to 66%-70% body mass),
respectively. Some significant PAPE effects were observed that
resulted in significant reductions in 15-meter (16 yd) sprint times by
Williams and colleagues (152) and Winwood and colleagues (160).
However, the significant PAPE benefit for Winwood and colleagues
(160) was only observed using 75% body mass loads and at 12
minutes after completing the sled pulls. The studies therefore
suggest that sled pulls with loads between 66% and 75% of body
mass have the potential to produce 15-meter sprint PAPE effects in a
variety of running athletes.
