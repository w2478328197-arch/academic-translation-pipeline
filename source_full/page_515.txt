The stress and injury model is a theoretical framework that attempts
to explain the effects of pre-injury psychological factors on athletic
injury (3). Revised by Williams and Andersen (68), the model
suggests that three unique factors can influence an athlete’s stress
response, separately or together, contributing to an athlete’s risk and
frequency of injury (figure 9.4). It includes athletes’
personality factors such as state or trait anxiety levels, mood
states, and perceived locus of control of situations and events;
history of stressors such as life events, academic stressors,
daily hassles, and previous injury history; and
coping resources such as social support, stress management
techniques, mental skills, and nutritional and lifestyle habits.
When a potentially stressful athletic situation occurs during
training, practice, or competition, these factors may affect how
athletes respond to the situation, either positively or negatively. For
example, an athlete with a history of high stress, a perfectionistic
personality, and anxiety is likely to negatively evaluate an injury. In
this case, the situation is more stressful and the athlete will likely
exhibit greater physiological activation and attentional concerns as
compared to individuals with opposite characteristics (68). Fear of
reinjury, anxiety or rumination about playing status, and inadequate
performance compared to previous standards are common thoughts
expressed by injured athletes (14, 59, 60). Appropriate interventions
that involve techniques such as cognitive restructuring (e.g.,
reframing negative thinking patterns), and relaxation techniques
(e.g., deep nasal breathing or visualization) can positively influence
the stress response and mitigate the risk of future injury (3).
Conversely, a resilient athlete (one who has overcome past injuries
and has coping skills and social support) will likely have better
adherence to rehabilitation programs and more positive outcomes
(68, 69).
