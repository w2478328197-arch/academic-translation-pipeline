Other notable differences exist between type I and II fibers. For
instance, type I fibers have more contacts with capillaries compared
to type II fibers (5, 19, 23, 34), and this feature (along with smaller
cellular diameters and more intracellular myoglobin) yields a higher
oxygen diffusion potential that favors aerobic metabolism. A 2021
research study using sophisticated molecular approaches has also
unveiled that the content of hundreds of structural and metabolic
proteins differs between type I, IIa, and IIx fibers (30), and these
findings suggest that these appreciable differences contribute to the
unique fiber type characteristics discussed in this paragraph. Based
on these differences, it is not surprising that postural muscles, such
as the soleus, have a high composition of type I fibers, whereas
larger locomotor muscles, such as the quadriceps muscles, have a
mixture of both type I and type II fibers to enable both low and high
power output activities (such as jogging and sprinting, respectively).
