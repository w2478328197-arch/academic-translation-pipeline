The types of motor units recruited for a given activity are
determined by the physiological demand imposed by that activity
over a given unit of time (table 1.2). For an activity such as slower
long-distance running, slow-twitch motor units are predominantly
engaged in working muscles given that physiological demand is not
relatively high over time and these motor units possess a heightened
endurance capacity and resistance to fatigue. Sprints at the end of a
race that impose a relatively higher physiological demand, however,
require the additional activation of fast-twitch motor units. If an
activity requires near-maximal contractions over very short time
periods, such as those observed with the power clean, most of the
motor units in the involved muscle(s) are recruited and the fast-
twitch motor units make more of a significant contribution to the
effort. Electromyography estimates have indicated that maximal
voluntary contractions with higher intensity weightlifting typically
lead to approximately 90% or more activation of available motor
units in the involved muscle(s) (7). However, some caveats must be
considered with this estimation. First, the activation of the entire
motor neuron pool is probably not possible in untrained people (9,
10, 18). Moreover, although large fast-twitch units may be recruited
if the effort is substantial, it is probably not possible to activate them
at a high enough frequency under most circumstances for maximal
force to be realized. These details aside, it is important to appreciate
that motor unit activation varies regarding different types of physical
activity.
