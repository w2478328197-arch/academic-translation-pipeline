While women have lower baseline testosterone levels, exercise can
still influence their hormone levels.
Moreover, individual variations exist among women, with some
producing higher concentrations of adrenal androgens. A study
comparing active women with inactive controls found differences in
baseline testosterone levels, indicating that regular exercise might
influence these levels (15). Despite these findings, other research
has not consistently demonstrated changes in serum testosterone
concentrations in women with training (23, 51, 56, 81, 134),
highlighting the complexity of hormonal responses to resistance
exercise in women.
While testosterone levels in women are lower, the dynamic
interaction between testosterone, SHBG, DHT, and androgen
receptors facilitates significant responses to resistance exercise.
These interactions can include muscle mass and strength increases,
essential for physical health and metabolic function. The response of
androgen receptors to even small increases in testosterone and the
compensatory role of other androgens like DHT emphasize the
complex but critical nature of androgens in female physiology and
resistance exercise.
An interplay between free and total testosterone is critical for
optimizing exercise outcomes. Free testosterone, which represents
only 0.5% to 2% of total testosterone, is vital for direct interaction
with muscle tissues. Younger men generally exhibit higher
postexercise increases in free testosterone compared to older men,
indicating a greater possibility for muscle tissue interaction (82).
According to the free hormone hypothesis, the free, unbound
hormone interacts with target tissues. However, bound hormones
may also enhance hormone delivery to these tissues, potentially
benefiting younger individuals more significantly (19). Due to
women’s relatively low total testosterone levels, changes in
hormone-binding proteins could substantially regulate hormone
activity, suggesting that these binding proteins might act as bioactive
hormones (120).
