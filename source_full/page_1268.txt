As well as enhancing nonvoluntary neuromuscular outputs, the
SSC serves a protective function within the myotendinous structures
of the musculoskeletal system. The Golgi tendon organ is
encapsulated within the serial components of the myotendinous
junction. This is a proprioceptive mechanism that detects the rate
and magnitude of force being applied to the respective tendon and
will cause an inverse stretch reflex to occur past a tensile threshold,
which immediately inhibits the excitatory signal through the motor
neuron to the motor unit. Progressive plyometric training serves to
desensitize the Golgi tendon organ, thus increasing the excitability
potential of the motor unit for increased performance gains.
The SSC combines mechanical and neurophysiological
mechanisms and is the basis of plyometric exercise. A
rapid eccentric muscle action stimulates the stretch
reflex and storage of elastic energy, which results in an
increase in the force produced during the subsequent
concentric action.
