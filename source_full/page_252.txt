Adaptations in the Endocrine System
From Resistance Training
Resistance training affects muscles and connective tissues and
significantly affects the other physiological systems, including the
endocrine system. This type of training also trains the endocrine
glands, with the gland’s involvement dependent on the demands of
the motor units activated during exercise. The endocrine system’s
adaptations to resistance training include changes in the following:
Hormone synthesis and storage
Hormone transport via binding proteins
Clearance and degradation rates of hormones
Fluid shifts from blood to tissues during exercise
Hormone–receptor binding affinity
The number of tissue receptors
Glandular cell changes
The intensity of signals sent to cell nuclei by hormone–
receptor complexes
Cellular interactions influencing muscle protein production
Hormones are secreted to maintain homeostasis, regulating
physiological functions during both the acute stress of a workout and
chronic stress from long-term training, which activates these
homeostatic mechanisms (17, 33, 34, 41, 49, 56, 125, 126, 130).
These adaptations might lead to subtle changes in resting hormone
levels, demonstrating the dynamic responsiveness of the endocrine
system to physical training.
