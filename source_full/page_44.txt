The junction between a motor neuron (nerve cell) and the
muscle fibers it innervates is called the motor end plate, or, more
often, the neuromuscular junction (figure 1.4). Each muscle cell
has only one neuromuscular junction, although a single motor
neuron innervates many muscle fibers, sometimes hundreds or even
thousands. A motor neuron and the muscle fibers it innervates are
called a motor unit. All the muscle fibers of a motor unit contract
together when they are stimulated by the motor neuron.
The interior structure of a muscle fiber is depicted in figure 1.5.
The sarcoplasm, which is the cytoplasm of a muscle fiber, is an
aqueous medium through which the exchange of ions (sodium,
potassium, 
calcium) 
and 
metabolites 
(ADP, 
ATP, 
inorganic
phosphate) occur to and from different organelles. Not only is the
sarcoplasm of critical importance to muscle fibers because of this
role, but intracellular organelles and structures are also suspended in
the sarcoplasm. These organelles and structures include the
contractile apparatus constructed of thick and thin protein filaments,
other proteins including metabolic enzymes and signaling proteins,
stored glycogen and fat particles, ribosomes that facilitate protein
synthesis, and specialized organelles such as mitochondria and the
sarcoplasmic reticulum.
