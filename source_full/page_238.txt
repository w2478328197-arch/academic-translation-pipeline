Importance of Recruitment of Muscle
Tissue
Henneman’s size principle shows that as the resistance increases
(i.e., heavier loads), more and larger muscle fibers are recruited to
meet the increased force demand. This principle outlines the
recruitment order of motor units—from smaller, slow-twitch fiber
motor units to larger, fast-twitch fiber motor units. The heavier the
resistance, the greater the number and size of motor units recruited
in a muscle to produce force (77). While anabolic and catabolic
hormones maintain homeostatic balance by interacting with all cells,
stress causes hormonal changes that reflect the body’s need to meet
new metabolic demands and recovery needs. However, only
recruited motor units adapt to the training stimulus and are affected
by the endocrine system’s increase in anabolic hormones, likely due
to the increased activation of more receptors. Interestingly, and of
importance, increased catabolic hormone responses to stress or
distress can affect all tissues even if not recruited for exercise
performance. A catabolic state is highly detrimental for an athlete,
especially when cortisol levels remain chronically elevated. Combined
with the presence of reactive oxygen species (free radicals)
generated by the body’s inflammatory response, this condition
significantly impairs recovery and hampers performance (77).
