The athlete’s arm
movement is
abnormally short and
tight.
Misunderstanding
of natural arm
swing
Instruct the athlete to either (a) drive the
elbow down and back or (b) pull the hands
down and back to simulate pulling on a
rope. In addition, cue the athlete to allow
the hands to fully break the waist while also
allowing the arms to recover at midline of
the body (invisible line from nose through
navel).
The athlete is holding

dorsal muscles and

Misunderstanding
of movement
Instruct the athlete to keep the head in line
with the spine; the torso and head should
rise at the same rate during the
acceleration and transition phases of the
sprint.
The athlete jumps the
first step or steps over
the knee of the stance
leg.
Push-off angle too
high; upward
thrust too steep
Instruct the athlete to initiate movement by
driving through the ground and allow the
swing leg to horizontally cut the stance leg
shin (i.e., ankle crossover), rather than
stepping over the stance leg (i.e., knee
crossover). In addition, cue the athlete to
keep the foot of the swing leg close to the
ground to set up a proper acceleration
phase.
The athlete gets into an
upright posture
prematurely.
Inadequate push-
off force; improper
carriage of head
Instruct the athlete to continue pushing
into the ground while maintaining a natural
trunk lean. Also cue the athlete to keep the
head in line with the spine; lifting the head
may result in a sudden lifting of the torso,
ultimately minimizing acceleration patterns.
MAXIMUM VELOCITY
The athlete is
superficially attempting
to maintain an
acceleration phase
when the shins are
clearly vertical.
Improper
understanding of
movement
patterns
Instruct the athlete that as the shins and
hips come up to vertical, so should the
torso and head. Encourage the athlete to
feel for the rise in the hips so that the joints
(shoulders to hips to ankles) stay stacked
or in line. This position allows for the
proper transmission of forces into the
running surface.
