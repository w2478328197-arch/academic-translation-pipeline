Heatstroke
Heatstroke, a progression of heat exhaustion, is considered a
medical emergency (12, 58). Heatstroke occurs from intense or long-
term 
exercise 
and 
is 
characterized 
by 
body 
temperature
dysregulation greater than heat exhaustion. Signs and symptoms of
heatstroke include rapid heart rate, hypotension, sweating with
possible evaporation of sweat by time of incident, hyperventilation,
altered mental state, diarrhea, seizure, and coma (12, 58).
Immediate recognition of heatstroke is vital to preventing lethal
consequences; core body temperatures in excess of 109.4 °F (43 °C)
have been associated with death. Immediate total body cooling
measures must be implemented such as total body immersion in ice.
Monitoring of core body temperature is paramount to appropriately
responding to heatstroke. Contacting emergency medical services is
indicated in cases where medical staffing is not present.
Prevention strategies include proper acclimation to training in hot
environments and ensuring proper hydration before, during, and
after training. A key element of prevention resides in recognizing
that heat illness exists on a continuum of events all related to
dysregulation of the body’s thermoregulatory capacity. As such,
heatstroke is likely to be preceded by heat cramps, syncope, and
heat exhaustion. Recognizing the presentation of heat cramps or
heat exhaustion before heatstroke is paramount to prevention.
