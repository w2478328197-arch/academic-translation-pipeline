Adaptation Limits and Receptor Sensitivity
When a muscle fiber has reached its capacity for protein
incorporation, or when a chronically elevated concentration of a
hormone 
causes 
overstimulation, 
receptors 
may 
become
desensitized or unresponsive—a condition known as receptor
downregulation; a classic example of this phenomenon is insulin
resistance, which can be caused by chronically elevated circulating
insulin concentrations. The reduced signal from the hormone
following overstimulation signifies a reduced ability of the hormone
to interact with its receptor or downregulation of the number of
receptors, which can effectively stop further cellular responses and
adaptations (37, 122). As such, receptors can adjust their affinity for
binding, and the number of available receptors can vary. These
changes affect cellular adaptation as significantly, if not more, as
changes in hormone concentrations. For example, heavy resistance
overtraining can lead to a downregulation of beta-2 receptors,
affecting their interaction with epinephrine and reducing muscle
contractile force (37). Exploring how receptor characteristics alter in
response to training and affect muscle adaptation is key to
understanding the complex interactions between hormones and
exercise.
