Heat Acclimation
It is well documented that aerobic endurance performances are
influenced by hot environments (55, 99, 100). Consequently,
undertaking training or competition in hot environments before an
athlete has acclimatized results in greater thermoregulatory and
cardiovascular strain, fatigue, increased perception of effort, and a
negative impact on performance (52) via elevated core and skin
temperatures, increased sweating, and loss of electrolytes. To avoid
these negative effects, athletes are advised to acclimatize prior to
competition in warm to hot conditions. Adaptations to heat
acclimation protocols include an increase in plasma volume and
thereby an increase in blood volume, an improvement in skin blood
flow sensitivity, earlier onset of sweating at a lower core body
temperature combined with an increase in sweat rate and
evaporative cooling, and a reduction in gastrointestinal distress (51,
101). These changes, along with many others, help provide
cardiovascular stability, lower core and skin temperatures, and a
reduced exercise heart rate. A more detailed description of the
adaptations to training in the heat can be found in chapter 6 (see
the section Heat Acclimatization).
Heat Acclimation Protocols
An athlete can adapt naturally to a hot environment by either living
and training in hot ambient conditions over a long period of time
(i.e., heat acclimatization) or training in an artificially hot indoor
environment (i.e., heat acclimation) (108). Heat acclimation
protocols are typically classified as short (<7 days), medium (8-14
days), or long term (>15 days) (50). Even though the majority
(~75%) of heat adaptations occur within the first four to six days of
heat exposure (e.g., heart rate and core temperature adaptations)
(101), it may take seven or more days for the remainder of the
adaptations to take place (e.g., enhanced core body temperature,
