With angular power, time provides another element that can be
manipulated, because performing more work does not necessarily
mean a higher power output unless any increase in performance
duration is minimized.
Impulse also has a rotational equivalent (angular impulse), which
is the product of torque and time (τ × Δt, where τ refers to torque,
and Δt refers to the duration of the phase of interest). As with the
linear impulse–momentum relationship, angular impulse is directly
proportional to the change in angular momentum (ΔL), and because
mass tends to remain constant, we can simplify this to angular
impulse being proportional to a change in angular velocity (Δω). As
with linear impulse, to move (rotate) faster, more (angular) impulse
must be applied, and we know that this can be achieved by applying
more torque, applying the same torque for longer, or a combination
of both.
Strength Versus Power
The discrepancy between the common and scientific definitions of
power has led to misunderstandings. For example, in the sport of
powerlifting, which involves high forces but relatively low movement
speeds, less mechanical power is produced than in several other
sports, including weightlifting (8, 14). Despite the discrepancy, the
sport of powerlifting is unlikely to be renamed. In all other contexts,
