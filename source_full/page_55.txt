1. The myosin head hydrolyzes ATP into ADP and Pi, both of
which remain bound to the myosin head. The energy provided
by this biochemical reaction results in a conformational change
whereby the myosin head is cocked into a high-energy state.
2. In its high-energy state, the myosin head binds to actin to
form the actin-myosin crossbridge.
3. The release of Pi from the myosin head catalyzes a
confirmational change in the myosin molecule, leading to the
power stroke, which is the pivoting of the myosin head that
pulls the actin filament toward the center of the sarcomere.
4. Following the first power stroke, ADP is released from the
myosin head and another ATP molecule binds to the myosin
head.
5. The actin-myosin crossbridge is subsequently dissociated, the
myosin head returns to its original low-energy state and
conformation, and the process is repeated with the hydrolysis
of the newly bound ATP molecule.
So long as calcium ions remain bound to troponin during muscle
activation, contiguous rapid rounds of actin-myosin crossbridge
formation, power strokes, and actin-myosin crossbridge dissociation
will occur, and this is termed crossbridge cycling.
Calcium and ATP are necessary for crossbridge cycling
with actin and myosin filaments.
Relaxation Phase
Relaxation occurs when the stimulation of the motor neuron ceases.
Calcium is pumped back into the sarcoplasmic reticulum through
specialized transporters that also require ATP, and the restoration of
low calcium ion concentrations in myofibrils highly reduces the
number of actin-myosin crossbridges.
