Task
The athlete initiates a linear acceleration from a two-point static
position and sprints to a predetermined distance (i.e., 5, 10, or
20 yards or meters) before braking and decelerating over a
series of steps as rapidly as possible.
Progressions and Regressions
Tasks can begin submaximally, preplanned, and over smaller
approach distances and thus velocity before increasing intent
and increasing approach distances and subsequent velocities,
and momentum. Deceleration drills can be further developed
through the use of generic and sport-specific stimuli. For
example, the “Stop at the Lights” game is a game-based agility
drill in which the athletes must react to the green (accelerate)
and red (decelerate) signals from the coach. Athletes race to
the end and are disqualified if they are unable to stop on
command. Additionally, drills can be further developed through
the use of auditory stimuli (i.e., “stop”) and partner or opponent
stimuli.
Variations
Practitioners can vary the initiation positions and planes of
motion (two-point, three-point, falling, false step, amplitude
jump, crossover cut, pivot, torso rotation, drop step, pop steps)
and develop a range of different deceleration actions to improve
