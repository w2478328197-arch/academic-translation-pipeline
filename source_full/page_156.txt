Overcoming Friction: The Impact of Load
on Static and Dynamic Friction
Friction is essential for movement control and provides resistance in sled-based
training for horizontal pushing and pulling. Researchers measured the horizontal
force needed to overcome static (μs) and dynamic (μd) friction for sled loads of
44.8, 90, and 136.2 kilograms (98.8, 198.4, 300.3 lb) (3). They found mean forces
to overcome μs were 206, 374, and 514 N, calculating μs (horizontal force ÷ sled
load, mass × g) as 0.47, 0.42, and 0.39. For μd, forces were 154, 289, and 415
newtons, calculating μd as 0.35, 0.33, and 0.31. Knowing μs and μd enables us to
calculate the force needed to start and maintain sled movement; this is analogous
to barbell mass in traditional resistance exercise. These ratios depend on the load
and surface conditions.
Friction
Friction is the force that resists sliding and is encountered when
one attempts to move an object whose surface is pressed against
the surface of another object. Exercise devices that use friction as
the main source of resistance include belt- or brake pad–resisted
cycle ergometers and wrist curl devices, but an element of friction
exists and contributes to exercise load from weight stack machines.
For such devices,
  FR = k × FN    (2.9)
where FR is the resistive force; k is the coefficient of friction for the
two contact surfaces; and FN is the normal force, which presses the
objects against each other (perpendicular to desired movement
direction).
The coefficients of friction for initiating and for maintaining
movement are different. All else being equal, it takes more force to
initiate movement between two surfaces in contact than to maintain
previously initiated movement. Thus, a friction-resisted exercise
device requires a relatively high force to initiate movement and a
relatively constant force after movement has begun, no matter what
