Physiological Testing
Although the strength and conditioning professional does not
typically order medical or laboratory tests to monitor the health of
his or her athletes and the risk of overtraining, it is helpful to know
what types of tests are available and what information they provide.
Numerous types of medical and physiological tests have been used
to help combat the onset of overtraining syndrome. In general, the
meaningfulness of these tests has been equivocal, dependent on the
subjects being studied, the training program being performed, and
the sport being monitored. A detailed description of all the tests
possible is beyond the scope of this chapter, but the reader is
welcome to examine some of the following references to get an idea
of what is available (9, 10, 19, 34, 36, 37, 39, 40, 45, 50, 54-57, 59,
65, 72, 78-81, 84, 88, 89, 98, 104, 113, 123, 124, 126, 131, 138). In
general, numerous potentially helpful variables can be assessed from
the blood (e.g., hormones and neurohormones, interleukins,
cytokines, markers of muscle disruption or damage, immune
factors), cardiovascular measures (e.g., resting heart rate, heart rate
variability, oxygen uptake, muscle oxygen saturation), skeletal
muscle measures (e.g., heat shock proteins, cell-signaling pathways,
receptors), neuromuscular measures (e.g., motor unit recruitment or
EMG activity), and orthopedic factors (e.g., joint inflammation or
inhibitory input). Data of this type can help the researcher and the
practitioner better understand the effects of overtraining and
perhaps eventually provide reliable biomarkers of a developing
overtraining syndrome.
