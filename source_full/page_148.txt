Sources of Resistance to Muscle
Contraction
The most common sources of resistance for strength training
exercises are gravity, inertia, friction, fluid resistance, and
elasticity. This section provides information on how we overcome
these forms of resistance during acts of daily living, exercise, and
sport. An understanding of the principles behind exercise devices
using the various forms of resistance can provide insight into their
effectiveness and applicability.
What Goes Up Must Come Down: Gravity
and Vertical Jump Performance
In sport, athletes launch themselves into the air. Jump height depends on takeoff
vertical velocity (TOV). To jump higher, athletes must leave the ground faster
because it takes gravity longer to reduce velocity to 0. Calculate jump height by
squaring TOV and dividing it by two times gravity. To jump 0.55 meters requires a
TOV of 3.285 m ∙ s−1. If calculating the TOV needed to jump a certain height, first
multiply the new jump height by two times gravity before calculating its square
root: 0.55 × 2 × gravity = 3.285 m ∙ s−1.
Gravity
The downward force on an object from the pull of gravity, the
interaction of mass and gravity, is equal to the object’s mass times
the local acceleration due to gravity:
  Fg = m × ag    (2.8)
where Fg is the force due to gravity (same as the object’s weight,
measured in newtons [N]), m is the object’s mass (measured in
kilograms [kg]), and ag is the local acceleration due to gravity
(measured in meters per second per second [m·s−2]). The
acceleration due to gravity can vary by geographic location.
