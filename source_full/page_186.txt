muscular fatigue experienced during exercise often correlates with
high tissue concentrations of lactate, lactate is not the cause of
fatigue (22, 27, 125). Proton (H+) accumulation during fatigue
reduces the intracellular pH, inhibits glycolytic reactions, and directly
interferes with muscle’s excitation–contraction coupling—possibly by
inhibiting calcium binding to troponin (57, 115) or by interfering with
crossbridge recycling (51, 57, 79, 115, 146). Also, the decrease in
pH inhibits the enzymatic turnover rate of the cell’s energy systems
(9, 78). Overall, this process of an exercise-induced decrease in pH
is referred to as metabolic acidosis (125), and may be responsible
for much of the peripheral fatigue that occurs during exercise (42,
156). Also, the role of metabolic acidosis in peripheral fatigue has
been questioned (130), and other factors have been reported to play
a prominent role in peripheral fatigue, such as an increased
interstitial K+ concentration and Pi that impairs Ca++ release (120,
139). However, evidence suggests that other mechanisms, such as
the simple hydrolysis of ATP (125) (equation 3.1), are responsible for
most of the H+ accumulation and that lactate itself actually works to
decrease metabolic acidosis rather than accelerate it (27, 125). See
the sidebar Lactic Acid Does Not Cause Metabolic Acidosis. In fact,
lactate is often used as an energy substrate, especially in type I
and cardiac muscle fibers (10, 108, 162). It is also used in
gluconeogenesis (i.e., the Cori cycle)—the formation of glucose
from noncarbohydrate sources, lactate in this case—during extended
exercise and recovery (19, 108).
Normally, blood and muscle have a low concentration of lactate.
The reported normal range of lactate concentration in blood is 0.5 to
2.2 mmol/L at rest (67) and 0.5 to 2.2 mmol for each kilogram of
wet muscle (muscle that has not been desiccated) (67). Lactate
production increases with exercise intensity (67, 129) and appears to
