also be considered regarding agility. COD and agility movements
performed at shallow cutting angles (less than 75 degrees) and
associated with shorter ground contact times (less than 250 ms)
(94) benefit from training similar to speed training with regard to the
physical demands but still require additional perceptual-cognitive
training. On the other hand, when a COD involves a more aggressive
cutting angle (equal to or greater than 75 degrees), the length of
ground contact time can often exceed 250 milliseconds (8, 48, 64,
36) due to the greater braking requirements. Therefore, one should
consider increasing emphasis on eccentric strength for braking and
maximal strength alongside the concentric impulsiveness required
for propulsion during the reacceleration. Additionally, due to the
eccentric–concentric coupling during the plant phase of directional
changes, reactive strength development is also a key training priority
(36). Figure 20.11 provides an example of how various requirements
(angle of COD or perceptual-cognitive requirement of agility) may
affect the GRF and ground contact times of athletes. Of particular
interest is the effect of various methods of changing direction (for
the same angle of direction change) on GRF and ground contact
times. The effect of method of changing direction can be seen in
figure 20.11: The 5-0-5 was performed either with a single-leg COD
(outside leg turn) or with a jump turn that can be performed in a
testing environment but is not commonly performed on the field of
play. Nevertheless, this demonstrates that the technique chosen
dictates the demands; therefore, if trying to elicit a specific
adaptation from an athlete, one should provide specific instructions.
