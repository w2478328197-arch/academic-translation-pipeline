However, such workouts’ relatively low metabolic demands typically
result in only modest, if any, increases in blood concentrations of
testosterone, IGF-1, and growth hormone. Nevertheless, slight
hormonal changes, combined with paracrine and autocrine signaling,
are generally sufficient to effectively signal via receptors and other
molecular pathways within tissues in the neuromuscular system to
stimulate growth and repair. Conversely, a workout that involves
8RM to 10RM resistances, short rest periods of 1 to 2 minutes, and a
higher volume of work produces greater metabolic demands on the
body. The metabolic stress is evidenced by significant increases in
blood lactate and a surge in both anabolic and catabolic hormones.
These hormonal changes signal skeletal muscle receptors effectively,
even with the recruitment of fewer motor units using lighter
resistances. Periodization strategies that incorporate a range of
different resistances over time ensure effective muscle activation
across various workout styles, where hormones help mediate
changes. Such workout design variations reflect the complex nature
of exercise-induced hormonal activation and its contribution to
adaptive changes in the body.
Hormone responses in the blood are tightly linked to the
characteristics of the resistance exercise protocol.
