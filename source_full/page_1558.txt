Altitude
Altitude is classified into several categories, ranging from sea level
(>500 m [1,640 ft]) to low (>500-2,000 m [1,640-6,562 ft]),
moderate (>2,000-3,000 m [6,562-9,843 ft]), high (>3,000-5,500 m
[6,562-18,045 ft]), and extreme (>5,500 m [>18,045 ft]) (39).
While the percentage of oxygen is the same at different altitudes
(94), the atmospheric pressure drops, causing a reduction in partial
pressure (PO2), which acts as the driving force for gas exchange in
the lungs (39, 94). This leads to a cascade of physiological
responses to compensate for the reduction in PO2. Subsequently,
aerobic endurance performance decrements upon acute altitude
exposure may begin to occur at altitudes as low as 700 meters
(2,297 ft) (145).
Acclimatization to altitude may occur between 12 and 14 days at
moderate altitudes up to 2,300 meters (7,546 ft); however, it has
been found that this process may take up to several months (145).
According to Wyatt (145), recommendations for optimizing
performance at altitude vary dramatically, from arriving immediately
before competition (24-48 hr) to 12 weeks of altitude exposure.
Many elite and sub-elite athletes train at altitude to produce an
ergogenic effect. In order to experience a benefit from this type of
training, it has been reported that an athlete must receive a hypoxic
dose of training ≥12 hours per day for a minimum of three weeks at
moderate altitude (approximately 2,100-2,500 m [6,890-8,202 ft])
(66, 145). Live high, train low (LHTL) is a method commonly
used by athletes seeking to benefit from altitude training. LHTL
requires individuals to live at moderate altitudes, between 2,000
meters and 3,000 meters (6,562-9,843 ft), and train at or near sea
level (66). This method of training allows athletes to simultaneously
experience the benefits of altitude acclimatization and training at sea
level (139). Thus, LHTL may potentially provide an ergogenic benefit
by allowing athletes to take advantage of the metabolic and
hematological adaptations experienced when living at altitude to
augment neuromuscular development at lower altitudes (39, 139).
