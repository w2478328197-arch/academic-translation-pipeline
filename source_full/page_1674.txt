Cold Exposure
Cold exposure, or hypothermia, occurs when the body’s
temperature drops below 95 °F (35 °C), causing the physiological
systems to reduce functions and eventually shut down (18, 50).
While mild hypothermia is manageable, more severe forms are a
medical emergency. Hypothermia affects all organ systems, and
early symptoms include shivering; pale, dry skin; and increased
muscle tone (18). Increased heart rate, blood pressure, and
respiration may also be present as the body attempts to promote
thermogenesis (18). Risk factors for hypothermia include exercising
in cold temperatures without adequate warming methods as well as
exposure to rain or cold water that results in wet clothing, which
may promote further heat loss.
Upon recognizing symptoms of cold exposure, immediate
measures for warming the body are required through environmental
temperature and use of clothing. Monitoring body temperature is a
necessity once hypothermia is recognized; core temperature less
than 89.6 °F (32 °C) may lead to cardiovascular failure (18).
It is important to recognize that although the body can
acclimate to training in hot conditions, there is little to
no acclimation to training in cold environments.
Prevention strategies include recognizing extremes in
temperature exposure and use of protective clothing to
help create a warming effect while training.
