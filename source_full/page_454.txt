Endocrine
The endocrine system is responsible for regulating physiological
responses and adaptations to the various stressors encountered,
including physical activity. In response to strength training and
conditioning, the endocrine system regulates muscular repair and
growth (58, 95) as the various anabolic and catabolic mechanisms
triggered by the endocrine system affect the ability of skeletal
muscle to generate force and to adapt to training (95). The
immediate, or acute, endocrine response to a training session
traditionally has been thought to be a greater catalyst for muscle
tissue growth and remodeling than the chronic changes in resting
hormonal concentrations (94).
While a large number of hormones perform a myriad of functions
within the human body, a much smaller number affect muscle
responses to training, and even fewer have been the focus of
research in athletic females. The primary hormones to have known
influence on muscle and strength development, which have been
studied in females, include testosterone, the growth hormone family,
insulin-like growth factor-1 (IGF-1), and cortisol. It is also
important to understand general function of the hormones involved
in the menstrual cycle, because they can influence training and
performance in female athletes and are found in much higher
concentrations than in males.
Testosterone
Testosterone is present in much higher concentrations in males than
in females, but females produce testosterone in small quantities in
the ovaries and the adrenal gland (52). However, resting
testosterone concentrations are highly variable among females,
which can account for the variability in responsiveness to resistance
training of absolute hypertrophy and strength (67), although relative
levels of hypertrophy and strength are similar between the sexes.
