Plyometric Mechanics and Physiology
Functional movements and athletic success depend on both the
proper function of all active muscles and the velocity at which these
muscular forces are used. The term used to define this force–velocity
relationship is power. When properly implemented, plyometric
training consistently improves an athlete’s ability to produce muscle
force and power (34, 59). This enhancement affects the impulse an
athlete can generate. Impulse, defined as the integral of force over
time, directly influences an athlete’s performance by changing
momentum. Specifically, an increased impulse leads to greater
momentum, which allows athletes to accelerate and decelerate more
effectively. These rapid changes in velocity are crucial performance
qualities across a wide range of sports. These increases are best

Mechanical Model of Plyometric Exercise
In the mechanical model, elastic energy in the musculotendinous
components is increased with a rapid stretch and then stored (3, 16,
35). When this movement is immediately followed by a concentric
muscle action, the stored elastic energy is released, increasing the
total force production (3, 16, 35). Of the mechanical model’s many
elements, it is the series elastic component (SEC) that is the
workhorse of plyometric exercise. While the SEC includes some
muscular components, it is the tendons that constitute the majority
of the SEC. When the musculotendinous unit is stretched, as in an
eccentric muscle action, the SEC acts as a spring and is lengthened;
as it lengthens, elastic energy is stored and released with
subsequent concentric actions. Figure 19.1 provides a schematic
illustration of the components of the SSC.
