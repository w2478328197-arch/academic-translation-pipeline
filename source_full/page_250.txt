Interpreting Hormonal Changes in
Peripheral Blood
Understanding exercise effects on the body goes beyond physical
observations; it includes reading studies that monitor biochemical
changes such as hormone fluctuations in blood, muscle, and
surrounding fluids (114). These changes provide insights into the
body’s stress response and adaptation to exercise. However,
interpreting these changes can be complex, because hormone levels
in the blood are just one aspect of a broader hormonal response
that also includes neural and nutritional factors such as being fasted
or not and if proteins and carbohydrates are ingested around a
workout. Increases in blood hormone levels generally indicate
enhanced release from endocrine glands, potentially boosting
hormone–receptor interactions in target tissues. Yet the actual
impact depends on the state and quantity of the receptors—whether
they are primed to bind hormones and initiate crucial processes
involved in protein synthesis, including activating pathways such as
mTOR (11, 13, 14, 66). Conversely, decreases in hormone levels
might suggest increased tissue uptake, faster hormone breakdown,
or reduced secretion.
Several physiological factors, including circadian rhythms, fluid
volume changes, tissue clearance rates, venous pooling, and
interactions with binding proteins, also influence observed circulating
hormone concentrations after exercise. These factors determine the
hormone’s efficacy in interacting with receptors and triggering
cellular responses, underlining the complexity of hormone function
and its role in exercise adaptations.
While increased hormone levels after exercise indicate heightened
glandular activity, they do not directly correlate with muscle gains.

hormone levels by, for example, using heavy 1RM to 3RM zones with
