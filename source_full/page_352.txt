Endocrine Adaptations
Just as endocrine responses to resistance training are needed for
muscle development (87), the changes in hormone production that
contribute to the body’s adaptation to aerobic exercise are equally
important (45, 46, 82, 108, 113). Testosterone, insulin, insulin-like
growth factors (IGF-1), and growth hormone affect the integrity of
muscle, bone, and connective tissue as well as assist in maintaining
metabolism within a normal range (40, 41, 84, 85, 158). The
endocrine adaptations following aerobic endurance training vary
according to the level of exercise intensity and duration, with some
hormonal responses increasing while others decrease, as outlined in
the following.
High-intensity aerobic training augments the absolute secretion
rates of many hormones in response to maximal exercise, although
trained athletes have attenuated hormonal responses to submaximal
exercise (103). However, the hormonal response of trained athletes
is similar to an untrained person when performing aerobic endurance
exercise at the same relative submaximal exercise intensity (108).
The greater hormonal response patterns to maximal exercise appear
to augment the athlete’s ability to tolerate and sustain prolonged
high-intensity exercise (170). When exercise intensity is very high
and exercise duration is very short (from 5-10 s), only “fight-or-
flight” changes in peripheral blood hormone concentrations occur
(e.g., epinephrine and norepinephrine concentrations increase) (86).
