greatly limits an athlete’s ability to make improvements. The obvious application
of this principle in the design of resistance training programs involves increasing
the loads assigned in the exercises, especially if the primary training goal is to
improve maximal strength. Other more subtle changes include altering the
number of training sessions per week (or per day in some instances) or
increasing training volume by adding exercises or sets, which may preferentially
stimulate a hypertrophic response (96, 143). Alternatively, varying exercise
choice, modifying rest periods between sets and exercises, or any combination
of these can result in an overload. The basis of the overload principle is to
provide the body with a unique training stimulus to facilitate the development of
the desired adaptations. Although an overload stimulus is typically thought of as
an increase of a given training variable (e.g., load, volume, velocity), a
reduction in these variables may also provide the opportunity for adaptation to
take place. For example, a reduction in intensity (i.e., relative load) allows for an
increase in the speed of a movement, thus facilitating the development of rapid
force production characteristics (i.e., speed–strength). Alternatively, a reduction
in training volume while maintaining (or increasing) intensity during a deload
week may lead to an enhancement in performance (figure 18.1) if a sufficient
stimulus (i.e., volume and intensity) has been applied previously. When the
overload principle is properly and systematically applied, and all other
components of training are considered (e.g., skill-based technical and tactical
training, conditioning, and external stressors), overtraining can be avoided and
the desired training adaptation will occur.
