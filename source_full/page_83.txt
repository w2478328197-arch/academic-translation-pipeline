Acute Responses to Aerobic Exercise
A single bout of aerobic exercise places a significant metabolic
demand on the body, especially the cardiovascular, respiratory, and
muscular systems. Repeated exposure to the acute stress of exercise
that occurs with chronic exercise training results in many changes in
the function and responses of all body systems. A basic knowledge
of the acute effects of aerobic exercise provides the foundation for
an understanding of the chronic adaptations that are discussed in
chapter 6.
Cardiovascular Responses
The primary functions of the cardiovascular system during aerobic
exercise are to deliver oxygen and other nutrients to the working
muscles and remove metabolites and waste products. This section
describes the cardiovascular mechanisms of these acute responses.
Cardiac Output
Cardiac output is the amount of blood pumped by the heart in
liters per minute and is determined by the quantity of blood ejected
with each beat (stroke volume) and the heart’s rate of pumping
(heart rate):
Q̇  = Stroke volume × Heart rate (1.1)
where Q̇  is the cardiac output. Stroke volume is measured in
milliliters of blood per beat, and heart rate is measured in beats
(contractions) per minute (17).
In the progression from rest to steady-state aerobic exercise,
cardiac output initially increases rapidly, then more gradually, and
subsequently reaches a plateau. With maximal exercise, cardiac
output may increase to four times the resting level of about 5 L/min
to a maximum of 20 to 22 L/min (1). Stroke volume begins to
