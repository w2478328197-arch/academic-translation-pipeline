Performance Preparation—The Warm-
Up
A warm-up period is now almost universally accepted as an integral
part of any training session or competition (10), yet until recently
there was surprisingly little published research to support the
practice (65) and even less on the optimal structure and content of a
warm-up (48). Essentially, the overarching goal of a warm-up is to
prepare an athlete mentally and physically for exercise or
competition (45). A period of preparation is needed because a body
at rest is unprepared for optimal performance. A period of warming
up facilitates the instigation of a range of acute physiological
responses that enable the body to cope with the specific demands
being placed on it (65). Taken together, these acute responses not
only enable the body to cope with these acute demands but also
facilitate responses that can potentially optimize subsequent
performance. While these processes occur across a number of body
systems, they typically can be divided into temperature-related
effects and non–temperature-related effects (10).
Temperature-related effects include an increase in muscle
temperature, core temperature (62), enhanced neural function, and
the disruption of transient connective tissue bonds (30), while non–
temperature-related effects can include increased blood flow to
muscles, an elevation of baseline oxygen consumption, and
postactivation potentiation (10). While passive warm-up techniques
(e.g., external heating of the muscles) may be able to generate
some of the temperature-related effects, active warm-ups (using
exercises or activity to elicit the required response) are generally
preferred and advised because they are able to stimulate both the
temperature- and non–temperature-related effects and can also
affect the metabolic pathways associated with the upcoming activity
