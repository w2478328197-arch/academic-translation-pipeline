regarding COD ability is the orientation of the body leading into the
deceleration, through the transition phase as the athlete comes to a
stop, and then the positioning as he or she subsequently
reaccelerates out of the COD. Therefore, the positioning of the trunk
influences the performance of the COD. As such, it is a combination
of the ability to decelerate (high braking impulse), reorient the body
to face or partially face the direction of intended travel, and then
reaccelerate (high propulsive impulse) over short contact times that
truly determines COD ability. Additionally, the ability to perform the
COD requires the athlete to have the physical capacity to produce
high and rapid magnitudes of force, tolerate the high loads
associated with braking, and adopt favorable postures that are
associated with braking and propulsion (22, 35).
Considering the requirements for rapidly changing momentum,
increased muscle mass in combination with decreased body fat (i.e.,
improving functional mass) has been regarded as a predictor of COD
performance when assessed by the T-test in elite basketball players
(23). Other anthropometrics and their relationship to COD ability are
not discussed here, because aspects such as height and limb length
are not modifiable by the strength and conditioning professional.
Instead, body center of mass height is related to improved lateral
COD performance (89), and this can be actively targeted during
technique modification training (22, 35).

increased hip extension velocity (rapid application of force by the hip
extensors), low center of mass height, wide lateral foot plants for
perpendicular force application, increased braking impulse and
