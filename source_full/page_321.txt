The fact that articular cartilage gets its nutrient supply via
diffusion from synovial fluid provides a link for joint mobility to joint
health. Movement about a joint creates changes in pressure in the
joint capsule that drive nutrients from the synovial fluid toward the
articular cartilage of the joint (156). Immobilization of a joint
prevents proper diffusion of oxygen and essential nutrients
throughout the joint. This results in the death of the healthy cells
within cartilage, called chondrocytes, and a resorption of the
cartilage matrix (167). Current understanding indicates that human
cartilage undergoes atrophy, or thinning, when external loading is
removed (e.g., postoperative immobilization and paraplegia).
However, the effect that increased external loading has on average
cartilage thickness remains to be fully elucidated (50). In any case, it
is likely that genetics plays a greater role in determining cartilage
morphology.
