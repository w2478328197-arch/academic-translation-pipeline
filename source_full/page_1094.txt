The degree of resistance encountered with this type of training is
dictated by altering the angle of the suspension trainer’s straps,
effectively changing the gravitational pull as a percentage of the
individual’s body weight (90, 112). Generally, as the athlete’s center
of mass moves farther away from the anchor point, the lower the
amount of resistance to which the athlete is exposed (90). For
example, Melrose and Dawes (90) report that when the inverted row
is performed with the straps at a 75-degree angle from the ground
(i.e., center of mass closer to the attachment point) the intensity of
the exercise will correspond to 79.4% ± 2.1% of body weight. If,
however, the angle is changed to 30 degrees from the ground (i.e.,
center of mass is farther away from the attachment point), the
resistance encountered is reduced to 37.4% ± 21.5% of body
weight. It is clear based on this study that suspension training is
limited in its ability to provide resistances greater than body weight;
however, it is important to note that resistance can be added by
wearing a weighted vest during suspension training, thus increasing
the potential training stimulus (30, 36).
While there are limitations to the amount of load that can be
applied when performing suspension training, this type of training
has several benefits (2, 30, 36, 90), including the following:
Versatility. Can be used with a wide range of exercises that
target a variety of muscle groups
Portability. Is easily transported when traveling and can be
used indoors or outdoors
Space-efficient. Requires minimal storage space
Cost-effective. Relatively inexpensive compared to other
resistance training methods
Scalability. Can be adjusted to increase or decrease difficulty
through changing body position or angle
Balance and coordination. Challenges proprioception and body
awareness
Muscle activation. Changes in body position can alter muscle
activation patterns
