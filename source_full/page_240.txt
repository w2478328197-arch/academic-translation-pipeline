Role of Receptors in Mediating
Hormonal Changes
The effectiveness of hormonal signals depends entirely on the
presence of hormone-specific receptors on or in the target cells. This
ensures that each hormone only affects its intended cells and tissues
(those that express the specific receptors). Such specificity is crucial
for maintaining precise hormonal regulation within the body. Since
many hormones cannot directly cross the cell membrane, they rely
on binding to membrane receptors that cause secondary
messengers to transmit signals internally, including reaching the
DNA in the cell’s nuclei. These receptors, located either in the cell
membrane for polypeptide hormones or within the cytosol for
steroid and thyroid hormones, allow various cells, from muscle fibers
to immune and brain cells, to respond to hormonal signals.
A fundamental principle in endocrinology, often described through
the lock-and-key model (24), explains how a hormone (the key)
must precisely match a receptor (the lock) to trigger a response
(figure 4.2). However, this model oversimplifies the complexity of
hormone–receptor interactions. For example, cross-reactivity
allows receptors to engage with hormones other than their primary
matches, and allosteric binding can modify how a receptor
responds to its primary hormone (such as blocking binding to the
receptor and thus signaling). Additionally, receptors might have
allosteric sites where nonhormonal substances can enhance or
reduce the hormonal response. Some hormones, like GH, might even
need to aggregate into specific forms (multiple hormones bound
together in a “cluster”) to effectively signal through their receptors,
as shown in studies using the tibial line assay (40), indicating that
specific molecular configurations are essential for their activity.
