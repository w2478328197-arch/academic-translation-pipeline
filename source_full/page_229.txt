Historical Perspective on Interest in the
Endocrine System
The significance of the endocrine system in strength and
conditioning gained prominence with the rise of anabolic drug use in
the 1940s and 1950s (7, 58). Fahey and colleagues were the first to
investigate how resistance exercises affected endogenous hormone
concentrations, specifically testosterone, across different sexes,
ages, and training statuses, paving the way for ongoing research
into hormonal responses to resistance exercise and training (23). In
1988, a series of reviews were published from the first symposium
on the physiology of resistance exercise and training at the American
College of Sports Medicine’s annual meeting. As part of that
symposium set of papers, Kraemer (97) overviewed the sparse
number of studies that had been done on the various hormonal
responses to resistance exercise and training. This stimulated a line
of hormonal research that continues today to explore further the
mechanisms behind these acute responses and training adaptations
(46). Now the body of literature consists of thousands of studies on
this topic.
