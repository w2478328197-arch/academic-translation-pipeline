Fiber types are also delineated by differences in fatigue resistance
and force production capabilities, and this can be due to small to
moderate differences in cellular morphology, intracellular protein
content, and organelle distribution. For instance, type I fibers are
generally more fatigue resistant relative to type II fibers, and type I
fibers have a higher capacity to produce ATP via aerobic metabolism.
This difference can be largely attributed to mitochondrial
characteristic differences between fiber types, because type I fibers
have a higher density and a more even distribution of mitochondria
between myofibrils relative to type II fibers (31). However, type I
fibers produce less overall force and yield a lower rate of force
development compared to type II fibers. These latter characteristic
differences are chiefly due to type I myofibers exhibiting lower
cellular diameters (and thus less myofibrils per cell) compared to
type II fibers (21). Additionally, myosin ATPase reaction rates differ
between the type I and II isoforms, wherein the type I isoform
exhibits a relatively lower reaction rate and thus a reduced rate at
which crossbridge cycling can occur (38).
