smaller, more gradual reductions in volume at each tier. Training
volume is systematically reduced while intensity and frequency are
maintained.

and Resistance Training
Resistance training is an important but often overlooked factor in
improving performance in aerobic endurance athletes. This may be
due to misconceptions that resistance training may increase an

muscle

and

performance, or the potential interference phenomenon that may
occur when resistance and aerobic endurance training sessions are
completed within a training program (i.e., concurrent training)

training can provide athletes, such as reducing muscle imbalances,
assistance with preventing overuse injuries, and recovery from
injuries (122), numerous research investigations (25, 32, 45, 82, 84,
109, 114, 119, 127, 135), meta-analyses, and systematic review
articles (1, 4, 10, 11, 17, 31, 37, 38, 65, 80, 89, 116, 143) have
demonstrated that it can also improve performance (e.g., time trial,
middle- and long-distance) across a number of aerobic endurance
disciplines including running, cycling, cross-country skiing, and
swimming, and performance-related variables such as strength,
power, running economy, and maximal sprint speed. For example, a
systematic review found that resistance training not only improved
running performance from distances of 1,500 meters (0.9 mile) to
10,000 meters (6.2 miles) but also improved muscular strength and
