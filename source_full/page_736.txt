Evaluation of Test Quality
Test results are valuable only when two key criteria are met: validity
and reliability. Validity ensures the test accurately measures its
intended attribute, while reliability guarantees consistency in
measurement 
across 
multiple 
instances. 
These 
fundamental
characteristics are essential in assessing the quality of a test and its
associated metrics. Both must meet acceptable standards for the
test to provide meaningful benefits.
Validity
Validity refers to the degree to which a test or test item measures
what it is supposed to measure and is one of the most important
characteristics of testing (2, 25). For tests of physical properties such
as height and weight, validity is easy to establish. For example, close
correspondence between the readings on a spring scale and the
readings on a calibrated balance scale indicates validity of weighing
with the spring scale. The validity of tests of basic athletic abilities or
capacities is more difficult to establish. There are several types of
validity, including construct validity, face validity, content validity, and
criterion validity.
To be valid, physical performance tests should measure
abilities important in the sport, produce repeatable
results (see the later section on reliability), measure the
performance of one athlete at a time (unless otherwise
specified in the protocol), appear meaningful, be of
suitable difficulty, be able to differentiate between
various levels of ability, permit accurate scoring, include
a sufficient number of trials, and withstand the test of
statistical evaluation. Given the choice between two
valid tests, consideration should be given to simplicity
and economy of test administration.
