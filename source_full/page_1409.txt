SSC actions exploit intrinsic muscle–tendon behavior as well as
the force and length reflex feedback to the nervous system (3, 4, 10,
18, 33). Acutely, SSC actions tend to increase mechanical efficiency
and impulse via elastic energy recovery, whereas chronically, they
upregulate muscle stiffness and enhance neuromuscular activation
(45, 55, 56, 82, 83).
Training activities aimed at improving SSC performance should
fulfill two criteria (47, 82, 83, 91, 98):
They should involve skillful, multijoint movements that
transmit forces through the kinetic chain and exploit elastic–
reflexive mechanisms.
To manage fatigue and emphasize work quality and technique,
they should be structured around brief work bouts or clusters
separated by frequent rest pauses.
In practice, a combination of progressive plyometric and heavy
resistance methods can accomplish these objectives (i.e., complex or
contrast training).
Spring–Mass Model
Exposure to strength and speed training may be linked to a rise in
the preactivation of the musculature used in sprinting (54, 56). The
onset of pre-tension may be related to an increase in the sensitivity
of associated muscle spindles. The improvement in the time needed
for feedback from muscle spindles results in greater muscle stiffness
