Consider the dumbbell curl exercise where forearm length is 0.30
meters (0.98 ft), the dumbbell is 25 kilograms (55 lb), and the lifter
is able to keep the upper arm tight against the body, preventing it
from moving as he or she curls it through a vertical displacement of
0.60 meters (1.97 ft) in 1.283 seconds. The (linear) work performed
by the lifter would be 140.64 joules at a linear power output of
109.59 watts. The angular displacement is 2.02 radians (or
approximately 116 degrees), and the average angular velocity (Δ
joint angle ÷ time) is 1.57 radians per second (or 90 degrees per
second). The average torque necessary to displace the dumbbell
through that range of motion is 59.1 newton-meters, although this
varies depending on the elbow joint angle where 27.7 newton-
meters is required at the start (43 degrees), which gradually
increases to 74.3 newton-meters when the elbow joint is at 90
degrees and then decreases to 50.2 newton-meters as the angle
decreases to 43 degrees at the end of the concentric phase. We can
calculate the required torque at a given joint angle by first
multiplying the mass of the dumbbell by the acceleration of gravity
