Momentum is defined as the product between the mass of an
object and the velocity of movement. During a sprint, an athlete’s
body mass remains constant. Thus, given the same time frame, the
only way to achieve a greater impulse is to generate greater net
force. This increase in net impulse leads to an increase in
momentum or a decrease in momentum, depending on whether the
athlete is intending to accelerate or reaccelerate or decelerate before
a COD or as an isolated action. In other words, a change in impulse
results in a change in momentum and is the cause of an object’s
movement.
Within human locomotion, the magnitude of the force coupled
with the length of time the force is produced during an individual
step is paramount to success. Changes in these forces can increase
or decrease the athlete’s momentum. For this reason, training should
focus on developing impulse—the area under the force–time curve—
in addition to RFD.
Power has not been discussed here because it is derived from
force and velocity. Therefore, power is considered a mechanical
construct and, practically speaking, it can be more useful to directly
measure the characteristics that determine power production (e.g.,
force and velocity), because measurement of power alone does not
give insight into the performance in a way that is fully useful to
practitioners. In understanding that force, RFD, and impulse are
direct, underlying characteristics that dictate explosive performance,
one does not require a more complex derived value to gain
additional insight.
