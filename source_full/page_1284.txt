Drill type
Explanation
Marching
Mimics running movements
Emphasizes posture and movement technique
Reinforces proper lower body movements for running
Ground contact
Prepares the body for impact and high-intensity drills:
Rapid pogo jump—not allowing the heel to touch the ground
(emphasizes ankle dorsiflexion with active front foot ground
contact)
Straight-leg skip or bound—emphasizes negative foot speed
(the rapid backward acceleration of the foot) into the ground,
minimizes knee flexion, and maximizes hip extension to prepare
for the impact of plyometric drills
Exaggerated form of reciprocal upper and lower extremity
movements
Emphasizes quick takeoff and landing
Mimics plyometric activities
Footwork
Targets multiplanar acceleration and deceleration mechanics
Prepares for changes of direction during plyometric drills
Examples: shuttle, shuffle, pattern, and stride drills
Neuromuscular
acceleration and
deceleration
Multijoint explosive directional changes with full-range
movements:
Forward, side, and backward lunge (similar to the forward
[step] lunge exercise but multidirectional)
Squat patterns (e.g., a tall reach into a deep snap-down then a
squat jump to a standing landing)
Note: These examples are specific preparatory drills that can be incorporated into the
RAMP phases of a warm-up for plyometrics; see chapter 15 for more detail.
Plyometric exercise programs for children should be used to
develop the neuromuscular control and the anaerobic skills that will
carry over to safer participation in sport and athletics, both during
childhood and as they advance to higher levels of competition. For
example, several research studies cite the benefits of using proper
landing technique as a method of reducing an athlete’s risk of lower
extremity injury (see the Safety Considerations section starting on
page 576 for details).
