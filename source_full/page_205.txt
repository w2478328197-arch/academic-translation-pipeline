The effects of training and supplementation on resting
concentrations of phosphagens is an ongoing area of investigation.
Aerobic endurance training may increase resting concentrations of
phosphagens (49, 99) and decrease their rate of depletion at a given
absolute submaximal power output (33, 99) but not at a relative
(percentage of maximum) submaximal power output (33). Although
researchers 
have 
reported 
indications 
of 
increased 
resting
concentrations of phosphagens (12, 127), short-term (eight weeks)
studies of sprint and six months of resistance or explosive training
have not shown alterations in resting concentrations of phosphagens
(11, 16, 147, 150). However, total phosphagen content can be larger
following sprint training due to increases in muscle mass (150).
Resistance training has been shown to increase the resting
concentrations of phosphagens in the triceps brachii after five weeks
of training (106). The increases in phosphagen concentration are
likely due to selective hypertrophy of type II fibers, which are
reported to contain a higher phosphagen concentration than type I
fibers (105).
Glycogen
Limited stores of glycogen are available for exercise. Approximately
300 to 400 grams (11-14 oz) of glycogen are stored in the body’s
total muscle and about 70 to 100 grams (2-4 oz) in the liver (137).
