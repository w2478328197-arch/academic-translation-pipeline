Cortisol is critical in energy balance, immune regulation, and
vascular integrity within skeletal muscle. It aids in increasing energy
availability and modulating immune activity during exercise. After
exercise, cortisol helps reduce inflammation and muscle damage, but
sensitivity to cortisol decreases within 24 hours to protect against
prolonged exposure. Athletes show a refined capability to convert
cortisol to cortisone, protecting tissues during exercise stress.
However, overtraining can impair this conversion process, potentially
inhibiting anabolic activities like muscle growth by reducing IGF-I
synthesis (27, 30, 91).
Despite its reputation as a catabolic hormone, cortisol is essential
for carbohydrate metabolism, particularly when muscle glycogen
stores are low (25, 27, 99). It plays a role in converting amino acids
to carbohydrates, increasing proteolytic enzymes, inhibiting
protein synthesis, and suppressing glycogenesis and immune
function (30, 91). Cortisol levels increase in response to resistance
training, particularly with high intensity or volume involving large
muscle masses. The acute hormonal response prepares the body for
future physical demands and aids recovery.
The resistance exercise cortisol response differs some between
men and women. Men generally experience higher increases in
cortisol from certain types of training protocols, such as high-volume
workouts, whereas women rapidly adjust their receptor sensitivity to
hormones after exercise. Such data indicate a sex-specific adaptation
to training stress (133). Emerging research shows that resistance
exercise can affect glucocorticoid receptor concentrations differently
across sexes, indicating potential sex differences in hormonal
response to training. These findings underscore the complex
interaction between cortisol and its receptors in regulating muscle
adaptation and recovery (31).
