Professional credibility. In an increasingly competitive field, evidence-
based practice enhances the credibility and reputation of strength and
conditioning professionals. By basing their work in scientific evidence,
practitioners demonstrate a commitment to excellence and professionalism.
A variety of reputable sources, such as the following, can be used to ensure that
assessments and practices are grounded in scientific evidence:
Peer-reviewed journals
Academic textbooks
Professional organizations
Meta-analyses and systematic reviews
Conferences and workshops
Evidence-based practice databases
In-house data collection
Screening for Fatigue and Injury Risk
Monitoring acute and chronic fatigue due to training and competition
exposures and associated injury risk factors can allow the strength
and conditioning professional to prospectively modulate training
programs to promote athlete health and performance. Although both
fatigue and injury risk mechanisms are complex and multifaceted,
combining quantitative and qualitative tests can provide valuable
insights for the strength and conditioning professional and inform
any requirements for acute adjustments to predetermined training
programs, particularly during the competition period.
Testing can be used to assess athletic talent, identify
physical abilities and areas in need of improvement, set
goals, and evaluate progress.
