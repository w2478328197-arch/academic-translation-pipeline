Because physiological functions are more closely related to
biological age than to chronological age, at any given time an early-
maturing child will likely have an advantage in measures of
athleticism (e.g., absolute strength) if compared against a later-
maturing child of the same sex with less muscle mass. Clearly,
physical differences in body proportions can affect the execution of
resistance exercises. For example, short arms and a large chest
cavity offer a biomechanical advantage in upper body pressing
exercises, whereas conversely, long legs and a long torso are a
disadvantage in squatting movements. These anthropometric factors
have implications for strength and conditioning professionals who
are attempting to standardize fitness tests or develop a resistance
training program for a group of boys and girls who vary greatly in
physical size. It should be noted that whether in a testing or training
environment, the practitioner should use child-size resistance
machines or exercises using body weight, medicine balls, elastic
bands, dumbbells, or barbells. In situations where practitioners are
coaching groups of young athletes, they typically will need to
account for variation in technical competency, maturity status, and
training experience to provide a training session that is individualized
wherever possible. When coaching large groups of children with low
coach-to-athlete ratios, individualizing one or two additional
exercises per session (based on results from simple field-based
testing) might appropriately account for individualization. Similarly,
training sessions that pursue a common goal (i.e., movement skill
development and muscular strength) but adopt differentiating levels
(e.g., technical difficulty, intensity, range of motion, movement
speed) can also enable practitioners to individualize training based
on the athletes’ ability.
