In brief, two primary hormones are released from the ovaries
during the menstrual cycle, estradiol and progesterone. During the
initial half of the cycle, menstrual bleeding occurs and then estradiol
peaks and drops. Progesterone rises and peaks during the second
half of the cycle, and there is a second but lesser peak of estradiol.
By the end of the cycle, both estradiol and progesterone drop to
their lowest levels (figure 8.1).
Estradiol
In menstruating adult females, the primary form of estrogen is
estradiol, which has been shown to directly affect the structure and
function of muscle, tendon, and ligaments, as well as contribute to
muscle mass and strength (37). Estradiol appears to have a more
protective effect on skeletal muscle (100) by attenuating the amount
of damage. To do this, estradiol may decrease postexercise
membrane disruption, act as an antioxidant, act as a membrane
stabilizer, and mediate inflammatory cell infiltration (53, 141). In
addition, estradiol may influence contractile properties of skeletal
muscle and muscle protein turnover (71). This supports muscle mass
development by promoting protein synthesis over degradation and
enhancing sensitivity of the response to resistance training (71, 72).
High levels of estradiol are not always beneficial because decreases
in power and performance can result, which can increase the risk for
catastrophic ligament injuries (37). Low levels of estradiol are
equally problematic, resulting in increased muscle cell death and
reduced skeletal muscle mass and strength (39, 87).
