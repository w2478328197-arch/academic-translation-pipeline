While these exercises have become commonly implemented as part
of the resistance training practices of many athletes (4), there is a
paucity of scientific research exploring the actual physiological
impact and efficacy of these exercises. As such, significantly more
research is warranted to understand the unique training stimulus
provided by landmine exercises.
Sled Training
In addition to the training studies incorporating sleds as a part of the
conditioning program, several studies have examined the acute
responses to sled exercise (72, 150, 152, 160).
Keogh and colleagues (72) sought to describe the spatiotemporal
characteristics of the heavy sled pull, using six resistance-trained
subjects with experience in the heavy sled pull. Participants
performed 3 25-meter (27 yd) sets of sled pulls with 171.2 kilograms
(377 lb), with 3 minutes of rest between sets. Several significant
differences were observed between the acceleration (the start
through 5 m [5 yd]) and maximum velocity (20-25 m [22-27 yd])
phases, with average velocity, step length, swing time, and a range
of segment and joint angles being significantly greater in the
maximum velocity than acceleration phase. The fastest trials also
had significantly greater step length, swing times, and trunk and
knee angles at certain positions than slower trials. These acute
responses would potentially underlie some of the mechanisms
contributing to the chronic improvements in sprinting performance
seen with heavy sled pulls (96).
