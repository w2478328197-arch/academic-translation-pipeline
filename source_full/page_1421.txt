Technical Errors and Coaching
Table 20.1 highlights many common sprinting errors as well as
possible causes and coaching corrections. Quite often, these errors
are the result of the misapplication of forces due to improper
coaching cues, insufficient mobility, or disruption to an athlete’s
normal gait caused by external interference. For instance, an athlete
may demonstrate overstriding due to a coaching drill (such as
reaching to successively longer marks along the track to gain speed)
or the belief that acutely increasing step length will allow one to
catch up to a competitor in a race. Regardless of the reason, the aim
of strength and conditioning professionals should be to promote an
athlete’s speed through the proper transmission of forces into the
ground that will optimize the athlete’s gait cycle.

Start
The athlete should attempt to distribute a balanced body weight through a
set position (blocks, three- or four-point staggered start) that uses segment
angles critical to produce forces necessary to deliver explosive forces from
the start.
Front lower leg angle should be approximately 90 degrees.
Rear lower leg angle should be approximately 120 to 135 degrees.
Aggressive extension of the hip, knee, and ankle of the front leg against the
block or ground should be observed.
After initially extending at the hip and knee, the rear leg should then
be rapidly flexed at the hip (to ~90 degrees), knee (to ~90 degrees),
and ankle (to neutral position) in preparation for foot contact of the
first step.
As the rear leg is driven forward, the opposite arm should also drive
forward, mirroring the range of motion and timing of the rear leg. The
range of motion at the shoulder of each arm is large.
