How Can Female Athletes Reduce Their
Injury Risk?
To help female athletes reduce their injury risk, strength and conditioning
professionals should do the following:
Recommend preparticipation screening by a sports medicine physician. This
should include identification of risk factors for injury along with
musculoskeletal testing.
Encourage female athletes to participate in a year-round conditioning
program that includes resistance, plyometric, speed and agility, and flexibility
training. The conditioning program should meet the specific needs of each
athlete and should progress in a periodized fashion that allows for training
adaptations to continually manifest.
Ensure that females learn, and can repeatedly demonstrate, correct
movement mechanics (e.g., jumping, landing, twisting, and cutting) within a
variety of environments.
Precede every exercise session with a general dynamic warm-up and a
specific warm-up using movements that resemble those involved in the
activity and that target the activation of key muscle groups (e.g., posterior
chain muscle groups).
Provide augmented feedback within training sessions to optimize skill transfer
and enhance biomechanics related to injury.
Mental Health
Mental health has gained increasingly more attention in athletes
with a focus on sport organizations and practitioners to take a
holistic approach when training athletes (33, 57, 77, 149). Athletes
are exposed to additional mental health risk factors (77, 149), and
female athletes in particular are at higher risk for certain mental
health symptoms and prevalence compared to male athletes (5,
179). Research suggests that female athletes may have poor mental
health outcomes due to an increased risk of certain disorders (e.g.,
burnout, eating disorders, disordered eating, anxiety, depression) (5,
75, 144, 149) and are subjected to increased appearance-based
pressure (8, 144), discrimination, and financial strain than male
athletes (179).
