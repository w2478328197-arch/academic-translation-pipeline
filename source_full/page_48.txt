FIGURE 1.5 Sectional view of a muscle fiber.
Hundreds of myofibrils (each about 1 μm in diameter, 1/100 the
diameter of a hair) exist in the sarcoplasm of one muscle fiber.
Myofibrils are the contractile machinery of muscle fibers, and two
types of myofilaments are highly enriched in myofibrils, including
myosin and actin. The thicker myosin filaments (about 16 nm in
diameter, or 1/10,000 the diameter of a hair) contain up to 200
myosin molecules. A single myosin molecule consists of two myosin
heavy chain proteins and four myosin light chain proteins. This
molecule also contains two head-like features (one being from each
of the two heavy chains), two hinge points below each head, and
two tail-like features that act to keep the two heavy chain proteins
bound. When examining a thick filament containing hundreds of
myosin molecules, the globular heads protrude away from the thick
filament at regular intervals, and the head-like structures form
crossbridges with actin filaments during muscle contraction. The
thin actin filaments (about 6 nm in diameter) consist of two strands
arranged in a double helix. Myosin and actin filaments are organized
longitudinally in the smallest contractile unit of skeletal muscle
termed the sarcomere. Sarcomeres average about 2.5 μm in length
