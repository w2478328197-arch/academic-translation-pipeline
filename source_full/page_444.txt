Avoid supine positions and exercises; switch to incline, side-

Continue pelvic floor exercises to assist in labor and delivery and

Third (in addition to

Modify activity based on signs, symptoms, and maternal comfort.
Continue to monitor for adverse signs and symptoms.
Avoid supine positions and exercises; switch to incline, side-
lying, or vertical positions.
Modify activities to decrease discomfort or symptoms of
neuropathy.
10 weeks
postpartum
Pelvic floor exercises (i.e., Kegel exercises) immediately after
delivery for at least 12-24 weeks.
Core stabilization exercises immediately after delivery for at least
12-24 weeks.
Gradual return to physical activity and sport-related activities.
Increase water intake.
With Cesarean deliveries, more time may be needed to restore
abdominal muscles to prepregnancy strength and stabilization.

Injury and Health Considerations in
Female Athletes
Female athletes face higher risks of specific injuries and health
concerns than male athletes, possibly due to anatomical and
biomechanical differences. Strength and conditioning professionals
should emphasize lower extremity biomechanics and proper exercise
techniques to address injury risk. Female athletes also experience
higher rates of sport-related concussions, and mental health
concerns can be notable. Sleep deprivation heightens their risk for
injury and impairs recovery; thus, sleep education is essential.
Additionally, pelvic floor dysfunction, especially urinary incontinence,
affects female athletes at a higher rate, often affecting their activity
level and risk of injury.
