The Impulse–Momentum Relationship
Male rugby athletes can jump between 0.37 and 0.44 meters (1.21-1.44 ft) (42).
To jump 0.37 meters, they need to leave the ground at 2.694 m/s. Impulse (force
× time) changes momentum (mass × velocity). Because mass does not change,
only velocity needs to change. To do this at 95.2 kilograms (209.9 lb), they need to
apply 256.6 newton-seconds of impulse. To do this from a propulsion phase of
0.232 seconds means applying a force 1105.6 newtons over their body weight. To
jump 0.44 meters (16% more), heavier rugby athletes (98.2 kg [216.5 lb]) must
leave the ground at 2.938 m/s, which requires 288.5 newton-seconds. They
achieve this by applying 7% less force over 17% more time.
Positive Work and Power
When we apply force to an object that is moving, we are performing
work. In a biomechanical context, work can be understood in a
simpler form as the product of force and displacement (how far
the object moves). This is often how work is described in resistance
training and is familiar to many athletes and strength and
conditioning professionals.
However, work can also be understood as the change in the
mechanical energy of an object. Mechanical energy is the sum of
potential energy and kinetic energy (equations 2.3 and 2.4):
  Potential energy = m × g × h    (2.3)
where m is mass (as described above), g is the acceleration due to
gravity (9.81 m ∙ s−²), and h is the change in height (or position).
This represents the energy an object has because of its position.
  Kinetic energy = ½ m × v²    (2.4)
where m is mass and v is velocity. This represents the energy an
object has due to its movement.
