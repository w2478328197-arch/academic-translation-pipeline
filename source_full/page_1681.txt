Periodization and the General
Adaptation Syndrome
When the total training program is correctly designed and
implemented, the individual can adapt and improve. This can closely
align with Selye’s general adaptation syndrome (GAS; figure
24.1) (117). In the GAS paradigm as applied to any biological
system, a stress creates an initial alarm phase, followed by a
resistance phase, and ultimately adaptation. If the stress is too
great, the system is unable to successfully resist the stressor and the
biological system dies. In the context of an athlete and a well-
designed strength and conditioning program, this sequence initially
produces decreases in performance, followed by performance
recovery, and eventually performance improvements. This assumes
that the training program permits the athlete to resist the stress;
otherwise, persistent decreases in performance occur. These
performance decrements can be either long term due to
overtraining, or short term due to overreaching (see the
Overreaching Defined section later in the chapter) (93, 108, 125,
126). Therein lies the problem for the strength and conditioning
professional: how to create the proper overload (i.e., training stress)
and how to correctly adjust the training load to permit the athlete to
recover and attain enhanced performance. When done correctly, this
process can be part of a well-designed periodized program (see
chapter 22). It must be remembered that both the GAS and the
concept of periodization are paradigms, or models, and do not
necessarily have to look exactly like illustrations such as figure 24.1.

periodization paradigms (130), and the concept that fatigue can
accumulate from resistance exercise programs (63) have been
questioned by some academics. However, the vast majority of those
