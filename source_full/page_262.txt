The body’s response to GH varies with resistance exercise, and
understanding these responses is crucial for optimizing training
outcomes. Initially observed in studies from the late 1970s to early
1990s, resistance training can acutely elevate GH levels in the blood,
particularly through exercises involving large muscle groups and high
intensity (100, 123, 130). Research has shown that the type of
workout—especially the intensity, volume, and rest periods—
significantly affects acute GH responses (75, 83). Short rest periods
combined with moderate resistance tend to produce higher GH
spikes compared to longer rest periods.
While resting concentrations are not typically affected by training,
the ability to do more total work or tolerate greater increases in H+
ions and resultant pH decreases allows for greater peak GH
concentrations in recovery. While general responses are similar to
those in men, subtle differences exist due to factors such as the
menstrual cycle and hormonal contraception, affecting GH levels and
their response to exercise. Yet the impact on training appears
minimal (79, 81, 85).
Resistance training affects the various molecular or aggregate
forms of GH, with evidence showing that high-intensity workouts
lead to increased production of the 22 kD GH isoform. However,
research using bioassays suggests that resistance training might
reduce the release of larger GH aggregates, indicating a shift toward
more newly synthesized GH and less storage in aggregates (60, 72,
104). This adaptation might reflect an evolved response in the
anterior pituitary gland, particularly in highly trained individuals.
