Aerobic Capacity
In untrained people, heavy resistance training can increase V̇ O2max from 5%
to 8%. In trained individuals, resistance training does not significantly affect
aerobic capacity (52).
Circuit training, high-intensity interval training, and programs using high
volume, high intensities, and short rest periods (i.e., 30 s or less) have been
shown to improve V̇ O2max (25).
Motor Performance
Resistance training has been shown to increase running economy, vertical
and broad jumps, sprint speed, cycling power, tennis serve velocity, swinging
and throwing velocity, and kicking performance (116).
Power development appears to be negatively affected more than
strength during concurrent high-intensity resistance and aerobic
endurance training. Häkkinen and colleagues (70) reported similar
dynamic and isometric strength increases following 21 weeks of
concurrent training or resistance training only; however, the
resistance training–only group showed improvements in the rate of
force development whereas the concurrent training group did not
match this increase. Kraemer and colleagues (94) also showed that a
resistance training–only group increased muscle power whereas a
combined group did not. The resistance training–only group also
increased peak power in upper and lower body tests while the
combined group did not. It appears that power development is much
more susceptible to the antagonistic effects of combined strength
and aerobic endurance training than slow-velocity strength (94).
Finally, the sequence may play a role in the magnitude of adaptation
(51).
