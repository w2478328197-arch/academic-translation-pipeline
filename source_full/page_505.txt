Mental Health and Strength and
Conditioning
Mental health is emerging as an important topic for strength and
conditioning professionals. Research published related to mental
health and athletes has accelerated from roughly 74 publications in
2003 to over 1,100 in 2023. This growth demonstrates increased
interest in the topic, but research outcomes support the need to
focus on building competence in strength and conditioning because
mental health affects performance (24, 62). In a study, student-
athletes were at risk for depression (22.3%), anxiety (12.5%), and
low self-esteem (8%) (62). While most research has reported higher
rates of positive mental health for athletes compared to nonathletes,
training for and playing sports are stressful (31, 47). Some of the
stressors include burnout from long hours of training; social identity
pressure to perform well; and meeting high expectations from
coaches, teammates, and self while attempting to compete with
other athletes. These stressors can affect mental health and hygiene
that can appear at subclinical levels in student-athletes.
What is mental health? Defining mental health encompasses
emotional, psychological, and social well-being (5, 47, 62). Also,
mental health is not just the absence of a mental illness. Challenges
to mental health are often detected when sport, training, and life
challenges disrupt normal self-care behavior. Coaches working with
athletes will have varied backgrounds related to psychological
distress identification and mental health management. Some
practical parameters for coaching education related to mental health
issues are needed.
Strength and conditioning professionals can observe signs and
symptoms of changing mental health in athletes. While striving to
enhance performance, a holistic athlete monitoring program can
address self-care, recovery, and optimization of psychological,
behavioral, and emotional factors (5).
