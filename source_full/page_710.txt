Adverse Effects
It appears that a dosage of 0.3 grams per kilogram body mass (136
mg/lb body mass) sodium bicarbonate ingested approximately 60 to
180 minutes before activity improves short-duration, high-intensity
exercise performance (97, 111, 150). Dosages above this amount
have been associated with unpleasant side effects such as diarrhea,
cramping, nausea, and vomiting. A more tolerable dose (0.2 g/kg
[91 mg/lb] body weight) has been shown to reduce these side
effects; however, research has revealed that this dose does not
improve exercise performance (139). It appears that a sodium
bicarbonate dose ranging from 0.3 to 0.5 grams per kilogram body
mass, 60 to 180 minutes before exercise, is optimal for improvement
of performance (97). Due to the severity of the side effects (which
may be experienced by some athletes even when ingesting 0.3 g/kg
body mass), many sport scientists recommend that athletes try
sodium bicarbonate supplementation during practice before using it
as a precompetition aid.
Sodium Citrate
Although sodium citrate is not actually a base, it can increase blood
pH without the gastrointestinal distress that is commonly seen with
sodium bicarbonate supplementation (211). It is believed that once
in the blood, sodium citrate actually breaks down into bicarbonate,
thus increasing the extracellular pH. As a result, sodium citrate
would help regulate intramuscular pH during high-intensity exercise
by the same mechanism as sodium bicarbonate.
