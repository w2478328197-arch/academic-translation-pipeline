Every facility should have at least one phone that is accessible to
persons in wheelchairs. This complies with the Americans With
Disabilities Act (3); it also provides safety in the event of an
emergency if the only person who can help is in a wheelchair. The
phone should be close to the entrance of the facility or near the
office area so that a supervisor can call emergency services as soon
as possible following an accident (2). The ADA also requires that
light switches, water fountains, fire extinguishers, and automated
external defibrillator (AED) devices are reachable and that safety
signage is viewable by persons in wheelchairs (3).
A weight room should also have double or garage doors so that
large equipment and machines can be moved in and out. However, if
the hallway or access point is too narrow, the size of the doors will
not make a difference (14), because an outside wall will have to be
removed temporarily when moving or installing equipment.
Ceiling Height
The ceiling should be high enough to accommodate any jumping or
explosive activities. This includes the height of the athlete plus the
space required for box jumps, vertical jumps, and weightlifting
exercises. A recommended height is 12 to 14 feet (3.7-4.3 m), which
should give enough clearance to allow athletes to comfortably
perform most activities (14, 18). Certain exercises, such as overhead
medicine ball throws, may require additional overhead clearance. In
scenarios when sufficient overhead clearance is not available to
perform an exercise, using a nearby outdoor field, fieldhouse, or
gymnasium should yield enough space. However, these decisions
related to session management should be initiated in the planning
and design process to account for the impact on additional traffic
flow in and out of the facility during training sessions.
