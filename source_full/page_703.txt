A natural outcome of nitric oxide production is increased muscle
blood flow. Therefore, increasing muscle blood flow is also a popular
claim that is made regarding arginine’s effectiveness. Despite the
common perception among athletes and consumers of dietary
supplements that arginine supplementation does increase muscle
blood flow, investigations into this area are in agreement that
muscle blood flow is not increased in healthy individuals following
arginine supplementation (203). Oral arginine supplementation has
also been purported to improve endurance exercise performance.
However, in research investigating arginine’s effectiveness in healthy
populations, it was reported that time to exhaustion, local muscular
endurance, and intermittent anaerobic exercise performance were
not improved as a result of supplementation (95, 142, 214). Due to
its inability to increase nitric oxide production, enhance muscle blood
flow, or improve several modes of endurance exercise, oral arginine
supplementation is not recommended for healthy athletes.
Adverse Effects
A majority of the scientific investigations in which arginine was given
to participants to improve exercise performance used a 6-gram dose.
While this dose of arginine was not effective in improving endurance
performance, this amount was well tolerated and posed no adverse
side effects. Oral supplementation at doses up to 13 grams is
generally well tolerated. The most common adverse reactions to
higher doses (13-30 g) include gastrointestinal distress consistent
with nausea, abdominal cramps, and diarrhea (45).
